Three Complementary Paths

Philosophy is an activity, not just a body of texts or knowledge. Therefore, learning philosophy involves more than reading the works of past philosophers. In addition to the history of philosophy, one should also learn about philosophical tools and disciplines. The text below provides some guidance on the three complementary paths in the study of philosophy. These three paths are not independent of each other: specific methods and tools always came into existence in the context of the work of a specific philosopher or philosophical school. For example, the phenomenological method originated in the work of Edmund Husserl in the early twentieth century. Specific disciplines also vary in “popularity” throughout history.

Methods and Tools

Philosophers have developed many methods and tools to help them think. They have developed rules for argumentation, logic, ways of criticising ideas (e.g. by identifying fallacies), thought experiments, etc. In addition, practising philosophers also need reading and writing skills. The following books cover many of these methods and tools.

  • Pfister, Jonas: Werkzeuge des Philosophierens. Stuttgart: Reclam, 2013. ISBN 978-3-15-019138-5. An excellent introduction. Each chapter has exercises with solutions. The list of further reading also explains where to go after reading this book.
  • Rosenberg, Jay F. The Practice of Philosophy. A Handbook for Beginners. Englewood Cliffs, New Jersey: Prentice Hall, 1984. 3rd edition. Pearson, 1995. (133 p.) ISBN-10: 0132308487; ISBN-13: 9780132308489.
    This very accessible book is aimed at beginning students of philosophy and is based on the analytic style of philosophy. There is also an excellent German translation of this book: Philosophieren: Ein Handbuch für Anfänger. Aus dem Amerikanischen übersetzt von Brigitte Flickinger. 6. Auflage. Frankfurt am Main: Vittorio Klostermann, 2009. ISBN 978-3-465-04069-9.
  • Williamson, Timothy: Philosophical Method: A Very Short Introduction. Oxford University Press, 2020. (160 pages) ISBN 9780198810001.
    From the publisher's book description: What are philosophers trying to achieve? How can they succeed? Does philosophy make progress? Is it in competition with science, or doing something completely different, or neither?
    Timothy Williamson tackles some of the key questions surrounding philosophy in new and provocative ways, showing how philosophy begins in common sense curiosity, and develops through our capacity to dispute rationally with each other. Discussing philosophy's ability to clarify our thoughts, he explains why such clarification depends on the development of philosophical theories, and how those theories can be tested by imaginative thought experiments, and compared against each other by standards similar to those used in the natural and social sciences. (…)
  • Baggini, Julian; Fosl, Peter S.: The Philosopher's Toolkit: A Compendium of Philosophical Concepts and Methods. 2nd edition. Wiley, 2010. (304 p.) ISBN 978-1-4051-9018-3.
  • Dennett, Daniel C.: Intuition Pumps And Other Tools for Thinking. W. W. Norton, 2013. (512 p.) ISBN 978-0-393-34878-1 (paperback, 2014).
  • Schönwälder-Kuntze, Tatjana: Philosophische Methoden zur Einführung zur Einführung. Hamburg: Junius, 2009. (192 p.) ISBN 978-3-88506-092-5
  • Hospers, John: An Introduction to Philosophical Analysis. 4th edition. Pearson, 1997. (282 p.) ISBN 978-0-415-15793-3.
    This classic introduction was first published in 1956 and went through several thorough revisions until the 1990s. (The second and third editions had over 500 pages.)
  • Damschen, Gregor; Schönecker, Dieter: Selbst philosophieren: Ein Methodenbuch. 2., durchgesehene und überarbeitete Auflage. De Gruyter Studium. De Gruyter, 2013. (340 p.) ISBN 978-3-11-031448-9.
  • Brun, Georg; Hirsch Hadorn, Gertrude: Textanalyse in den Wissenschaften: Inhalte und Argumente analysieren und verstehen. 2., überarbeitete Auflage. vdf Hochschulverlag AG, 2014. (352 p.) ISBN 978-3-7281-3645-9. This is one of the books recommended by Jonas Pfister.
  • deLaplante, Kevin: How to Write a Good Argumentative Essay: series of 12 videos (between 1 and 11 minutes long) that discusses how to write an argumetative essay; it uses a sample essays to illustrate the principles presented in the videos. (See also the course on Udemy.com.)

Disciplines

Philosophy has been subdivided into several disciplines such as epistemology, metaphysics, ethics, political philosophy, aesthetics and logic (which can be seen as both a tool and a discipline).

  • Brandt, Horst D., Hg.: Disziplinen der Philosophie: Ein Kompendium. Philosophische Bibliothek 666. Hamburg: Felix Meiner, 2014. (672 p.) ISBN 978-3-7873-2531-3.
    Covers disciplines such as analytical philosophy, applied ethics, epistemology, philosophy of art, logic, metaphysics, ontology, phenomenology, philosophical anthropology, political philosophy and philosophy of religion.
  • Pritchard, Duncan, ed.: What is this thing called Philosophy? Routledge, 2016. ISBN 978-0-415-83977-8. (394 p.).
    This textbook covers the following areas: ethics, political philosophy, aesthetics, epistemology, philosophy of mind, metaphysics, philosophy of science, philosophy of religion and the meaning of life.

In addition to these general overviews, there are of course many introductions to each of the individual disciplines.

History

Readers are spoilt for choice for books about the history of philosophy.

A History of Western Philosophy (Russell, 1945)

Russell, Bertrand: History of Western Philosophy. Routledge, 2004. (792 p.) ISBN 9780415325059.
Bertrand Russell originally published this book in 1945 and it has remained in print ever since its first publication. It is very well written but biased against certain philosophers. In his autobiography, Russell said the following about bias: But to my mind, a man without a bias cannot write interesting history – if, indeed, such a man exists. I regard it as mere humbug to pretend to lack of bias.

A History of Philosophy (Coppleston)

Copleston, Frederick: Greece and Rome (Volume 1), Medieval Philosophy (Volume 2), Late Medieval and Renaissance Philosophy (Volume 3), The Rationalists: Descartes to Leibniz (Volume 4), British Philosophy: Hobbes to Hume (Volume 5), The Enlightenment: Voltaire to Kant (Volume 6), 18th and 19th Century German Philosophy (Volume 7), Unitarianism to Early Analytic Philosophy (Volume 8), 19th and 20th Century French Philosophy (Volume 9), Russian Philosophy (Volume 10).

Frederick Copleston was a Jesuit priest and philospher. He published the first nine volumes of A History of Philosophy between between 1946 and 1974. The tenth volume on Russian philosophy was added in 1986.

A New History of Western Philosophy (Kenny, 2006-2007)

  • Kenny, Anthony: Ancient Philosophy. A New History of Western Philosophy, Volume 1. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2006. (364 p.) ISBN 978-0-19-875272-1 (paperback).
  • Kenny, Anthony: Medieval Philosophy. A New History of Western Philosophy, Volume 2. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2007. (352 p.) ISBN 978-0-19-875274-5 (paperback).
  • Kenny, Anthony: The Rise of Modern Philosophy. A New History of Western Philosophy, Volume 3. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2008. (376 p.) ISBN 978-0-19-875276-9 (paperback) / ISBN 978-0-19-875277-6 (hardback)
  • Kenny, Anthony: Philosophy in the Modern World. A New History of Western Philosophy, Volume 4. Oxford: Oxford University Press, 2007. (368 p.) ISBN 978-0-19-954637-4 (paperback) / ISBN 978-0-19-875279-0 (hardback)

Anthony Kenny wrote this four-volume history with the objective to be more accurate than Russell and more readable than Coppleston (Copplestone's ten-volume work which is otherwise very sound). The work has also become available in a single volume (paperback or hardback).

A Little History of Philosophy (Warburton, 2012)

Warburton, Nigel: A Little History of Philosophy. New Haven & London: Yale University Press, 2012 (2011). (252 pages)
This very accessible book contains 40 chapters about great thinkers in the history of Western philosophy from Socrates to Peter Singer. The Yale University Press blog has an excerpt from the chapter on Kant and a short interview with the author.

Other Resources on the History of Philosophy

Books

:
  • Grayling, A. C.: The History of Philosophy. Penguin, 2020. (720 pages) ISBN 9780241304549.
  • Kindler Kompakt Philosophie 19.Jahrhundert. Ausgewählt von Jan Urbich. Metzler, 2016. (206 pages) ISBN 978 3 476 04066 4.
    This book covers 36 philosophers from Fichte to Rickert, mostly coming from German-speaking countries. Most philosophers are covered on two to four pages; other philosophers, notably Hegel, Nietzsche are treated in more detail. Philosophers from outside the German-speaking world include Comte, Kierkegaard, Spencer, Mill, Taine, C. S. Peirce and William James.
  • Dasgupta, Surendranath: A History of Indian Philosophy: Volume I (1957), A History of Indian Philosophy: Volume II (1952), A History of Indian Philosophy: Volume III (1952), A History of Indian Philosophy: Volume IV: Indian Pluralism (1961), A History of Indian Philosophy: Volume V: Southern Schools of Saivism (1955).
    These volumes were published by Cambridge University Press.
  • Kołakowski, Leszek: Was fragen uns die großen Philosophen? Leipzig: Reclam, 2006.
    This is not a history of philosophy but a collection of short lectures in which Kołakowski discusses some important ideas of thirty philosophers and thinkers and asks about the relevance of these ideas to the current world.

YouTube playlists and videos about the history of philosophy:

  • History of Philosophy is a course by Arthur Holmes, who is described as a “Christian scholar and philosopher”. The course focuses on Ancient Greek philosohy. It has 9 videos that are roughly between 40 and 60 minutes long. However, a longer, more complete playlist of the course, consisting of 81 videos, is available on the channel of Wheaton College. This version, A History of Philosophy, also covers philosophy after Antiquity up to the 20th century.
  • Hans-Georg Gadamer: Geschichte der Philosophie is a sequence of 7 videos in which the German philosopher Gadamer (1900 - 2002) talks about the history of philosophy from Antiquity to 20th-century hermeneutics. The source is a TV series recorded in the year 2000.

Other Resources on How to Become a Philosopher

  • InterUniversity Research Master in Philosophy: a degree offered by the Universiteit Gent, the Universiteit Antwerpen and the Vrije Universiteit Brussel.
  • Carrier, Richard: How to Be a Philosopher, Richard Carrier Blogs, 03.08.2010.
  • Korcz, Keith Allen: What Is Philosophy? and Why Study Philosophy?. These two articles are primarily about academic philosophy. The first article compares philosophy and science, and distinguishes between its disciplines and its methods. (History of philosophy is treated as a discipline of philosophy.) The second article consists mainly of a list of links to articles and webpages about the value of philosophy.
  • Warburton, Nigel: Nigel Warburton recommends the best Introductions to Philosophy, Five Books, 18.07.2018, updated on 09.08.2020). (In August 2020 Warburton started a short Twitter thread on whether introductions to philosophy are really necessary. Some philosophy professors appear to disagree, claiming that readers can move straight to Hume, Mill or Descartes without an introduction.) The book recommendations are the following:
    • What Does It All Mean? (1987) by Thomas Nagel (a very short and readable introduction by a significant philosopher);
    • The Life You Can Save (2009) by Peter Singer (not written as an introduction, but its focus on the question how we should live and Singer's counterarguments to common objections make the reader think about a central issue in philosophy);
    • Justice: What's the Right Thing to Do? (2009) by Michael Sandel (a book about political philosophy that grew out of course at Harvard University by a philosopher who can bring philosophy alive);
    • Causing Death and Saving Lives (1977) by Jonathan Glover (a book on moral philosophy by an English philosopher who believes that your thinking about philosophical issues should make a difference to your life) and
    • The Grasshopper: Games, Life and Utopia (1978) by Bernard Suits (a slim volume about the nature of game playing written in dialogue form).
  • Student Philosopher: Where to Start with Philosophy?, a video by the School of Life. The video explains that one should lose the fear of the subject and accept that it is OK not to know everything. It also recommends four books:
    • Bertrand Russell: The Conquest of Happiness (1930)
    • Alain de Botton: The Consolations of Philosophy (2000)
    • Erich Fromm: The Art of Loving (1956)
    • Milan Kundera: The Unbearable Lightness of Being (1984)
  • How should philosophy be taught?, a discussion on Aeon started by Nigel Warburton on 3 March 2016. The discussion is connected to the opinion piece What primary school children can teach academic philosophers by Peter Worley, CEO of the Philosophy Foundation.
  • Appiah, Kwame Anthony: What does a philosopher do? (10-minute video on the Big Think channel).
  • Jones, Josh: Download 140 Free Philosophy Courses: Develop Critical Thinking Skills & Live the Examined Life, Open Culture, 31.12.2014.
  • How to get started with philosophy without getting overwhelmed quickly? is a question on Philosophy Stack Exchange with a dozen different answers.

    Short introductions to books that cover the history of philosophy and a number of important philosophers from Antiquity to Wilfried Sellars.
  • Diehl, Ulrich Walter: Was ist Philosophie?.
  • Sadler, Gregory B.: The 10 Best Philosophy Books For Beginners, Gregory B. Sadler, Ph.D. | Medium, 11.05.2018.
    Sadler recommends the following books: Plato's The Last Days of Socrates (which includes Euthyphro, Apology, Crito and Phaedo), Nicomachean Ethics by Aristotle, Discourses, Fragments, Handbook by Epictetus, Confessions by Augustine of Hippo, Consolation of Philosophy by Boethius, Three Philosophical Dialogues by Anselm of Canterbury (which includes On Truth, On Freedom of Choice and On The Fall of the Devil), Selected Writings by Thomas Aquinas (Penguin Classics, 1999), Meditations on First Philosophy by René Descartes, Vindication of the Rights of Women by Mary Wollstonecraft and The Genealogy of Morals by Friedrich Nietzsche.
