Was die Erfahrung aber und die Geschichte lehren, ist dieses, daß Völker und Regierungen niemals etwas aus der Geschichte gelernt und nach Lehren, die aus derselben zu ziehen gewesen wären, gehandelt haben. G. W. F. Hegel, Vorlesungen über die Philosophie der Weltgeschichte. Bd. 1: Die Vernunft in der Geschichte.

Reading the works of Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel (1770 - 1831) can feel like the philosophical equivalent of listening to Vogon poetry, but many later philosophical schools (e.g. marxism, phenomenology, analytic philosophy) would probably not exist without him. When reading Hegel, it is also important to know that certain terms have changed in meaning since the early 19th century. For example, “logic” in Hegel's Wissenschaft der Logik did not refer to what we now call (formal) logic but was based on the term logos from ancient Greek philosophy. Similarly, “phenomenology” (see Phänomenologie des Geistes) did not its current meaning, which resulted from the work of Edmund Husserl.

Introductions to Hegel

  • Beiser, Frederick: Hegel. Routledge Philosophers. New York, Routledge, 2005. (364 p.) ISBN 9780415312080.
    This book tries to provide a comprehensive introduction to Hegel's philosophy. Its Further Reading section contains a annoted list of references that are useful for beginners. Other general introductions recommended by the author are Charles Taylor's Hegel (1975), Stepen Houlgate's Freedom, Truth and History: An Introduction to Hegel's Philosophy (1991), Raymond Plant's Hegel (1973) and Ivan Soll's An Introduction to Hegel's Metaphysics (1969).
  • Römpp, Georg: Hegel leicht gemacht: Eine Einführung in seine Philosophie. Köln: Böhlau, 2008. (298 p.) ISBN 9783825231149.
    An introduction for beginning philosophy students. Its bibliography contains useful annotations and helps students find out which books are for beginners and which require familiarity with Hegel's philosophy.
  • Emundts, Dina; Horstmann, Rolf-Peter: Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel. Eine Einführung. Stuttgart: Reclam, 2002. (112 p.) ISBN 978-3-15-018167-6.
    Contains a short biography and then discusses the development of Hegel's system in his early years, the “system” itself, its metaphysical foundations, and the later works.
  • Schnädelbach, Herbert: Georg Wilhelm Friedrich Hegel zur Einführung. 3., verbesserte Auflage. Hamburg: Junius, 2007. (192 p.) ISBN 978-3-88506-352-0.
    Recommended as a thorough introduction by Römpp (see above), but not an easy read.     Schnädelbach starts with the basic ideas that one needs to understand Hegel's work, especially the dialectic way of reasoning. The rest of the book then progresses through Hegel's major works, starting with the Differenzschrift. Schnädelbach points out that Hegel's own introductions to his system make certain assumptions that are part of his system, and as a consequence there is no way into his system without taking a sort of leap of faith.The author also points out that modern Hegelians have reduced the Hegelian dialectic to a hermeneutic circle. He concludes that Hegel's attempt to create a philosophical system ultimately failed and this failure is largely connected to Hegel's stance towards the philosophy of Immanuel Kant.
