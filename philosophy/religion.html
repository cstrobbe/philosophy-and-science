Confused Thinking about Religion

Religion, theism and faith are three concepts that are often used synonymously. For example, the quote from Bill Maher at the top of this page, uses the word “religion” to refer to faith. This careless use of language is just one of several reasons why much thinking about religion and faith is confused. Wikipedia uses the following definitions:

Religion
a cultural system of behaviors and practices, world views, ethics, and social organisation that relate humanity to an order of existence. (Wikipedia, 24.12.2015)
Faith
confidence or trust in a person or thing; or the observance of an obligation from loyalty; or fidelity to a person, promise, engagement; or a belief not based on proof; or it may refer to a particular system of religious belief (emphasis added; “a belief not based on proof” is the essential part in this context). (Wikipedia, 24.12.2015)
Theism
the belief that at least one deity exists. (Wikipedia, 24.12.2015)

History of Religion

There are many books about the history of religion or specific religions, but few that offer a convincing theory of the origin of religion. Many such theories assume that religion or faith originates from the attempts of early humans to answer questions such as, “What happens after we die?” and “Who created nature or the universe?”. Such theories confuse the “benefits” or advantages of faith—i.e. the answers to such questions—with the causes of its original emergence. (This type of thinking sounds like an example of the fallacy known as post hoc ergo propter hoc.) There is no evidence that these later benefits, with which we are familiar, are also the causes of the emergence of faith.
