While waiting for content about philosophy, you can watch some YouTube videos (see below). Also check the list of Free Online Philosophy Courses on Open Culture.

General Philosophy

  • What is Philosophy? is a short video by Massimo Pigliucci.
  • What is Philosophy for? is a short video by The School of Life. The video explains that philosophy is relevant to us because we&emdash;“normal people”&emdash;don't ask big questions, are vulnerable to errors of common sense, are mentally confused, have muddled ideas about what makes us happy, and panic and lose perspective.
  • The Benefits of Philosophy and Reason is a 10-minute talk by Kenneth Allen Taylor at TEDx Stanford 2012.
  • General Philosophy: an introduction taught by Peter Millican to first-year philosophy students at the University of Oxford. Links to slides, and audio and video downloads are available under a Creative Commons licence at the University of Oxford's podcasts website. (There are 33 videos, each between 5 and 23 minutes long. The University of Oxford also has a lot of other content available under Creative Commons.)
  • Introduction to Philosophy: an introduction by Richard Brown. It starts with the question what philosophy is (first video) and then covers important philosophers from the presocratics to Kant. (13 videos, each between 33 and 70 minutes long).
  • Philosophical Techne is a channel by Mark Thorsby, a philosophy professor at Lone Star College in Texas. His playlists cover the History of Ancient Philosophy, an Introduction to Formal Logic, an Introduction to Philosophy and and Introduction to Social & Political Philosophy.
  • The School of Life is a YouTube channel started by people who are fascinated by the sort of questions we're never taught enough about at school or college: How can relationships go well? What is meaningful work? How can love last? How can one find calm? What's gone wrong (and right) with capitalism? The makers of this channel also say, We love the humanities, especially philosophy, psychotherapy, literature and art - always going to them in search of ideas that are thought-provoking, useful and consoling. The channel started in September 2014; the videos are typically between 2 and 10 minutes long.
    The YouTube channel is only part of this initiative. See also www.theschooloflife.com and www.thebookoflife.org.
  • The Partially Examined Life is a YouTube channel with podcasts (with the same title) and other videos about philosophy. The podcasts and videos typically focus either on a specific philosopher or a specific topic.
    One of their playlists is The Partially Examined Life: PEL guests: this is a playlist of videos from various YouTube channels.
    Partially Examined Life Citizens is a related channel. The playlist PEL Episode Clips contains over 60 videos.
  • Critical Reasoning for Beginners is a six-part course taught at the University of Oxford. Audio versions of the lectures and slideshows are available under a Creative Commons licence.
  • Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature is a course by Tamar Gendler, who is Professor of Philosophy and Professor of Cognitive Science at Yale University. According to the course description, Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature pairs central texts from Western philosophical tradition (including works by Plato, Aristotle, Epictetus, Hobbes, Kant, Mill, Rawls, and Nozick) with recent findings in cognitive science and related fields. The course is structured around three intertwined sets of topics: Happiness and Flourishing; Morality and Justice; and Political Legitimacy and Social Structures. The course consists of 26 videos of 40 to 50 minutes. Course materials can be downloaded from the Open Yale Courses website. (Yale Open Courses are mostly available under the CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 US licence.)
  • In Our Time is a discussion series on BBC Radio 4. Some of the episodes are about philosophers; some of these have been collected on this channel. Each episode is roughly 42 minutes long.
    An archive of past episodes is also available on the BBC Radio 4 website.
  • Richard Carrier: Is Philosophy Stupid? (on the Skepticon channel). This is a video recording of a presentation an the 6th Skepticon conference, which was held in Springfield, Missouri, in 2013. The slideshow, a summary of the slideshow and some links about criticism of philosophy are available on Richard Carrier's website.
  • Jules Evans: How philosophy can save your life, a talk at TEDx Breda, 2013. (cognitive behavioral therapy and Epictetus)

Books

A Little History of Philosophy (2012)

Warburton, Nigel: A Little History of Science. New Haven & London: Yale University Press, 2012 (2011). (252 pages)
This very accessible book contains 40 chapters about great thinkers in the history of Western philosophy from Socrates to Peter Singer.
