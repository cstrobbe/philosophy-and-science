General Philosophy

Philosophy: Definition and Benefits

  • What is Philosophy for? is a short video by The School of Life. The video explains that philosophy is relevant to us because we—“normal people”—don't ask big questions, are vulnerable to errors of common sense, are mentally confused, have muddled ideas about what makes us happy, and panic and lose perspective.
    See also: What is Philosophy – and what’s it for?, The Philosopher's Mail (no date).
  • Why We Need Philosophy is a short video by Philosophy Tube. According to Olly, the creator of Philosophy Tube, we accept too many things uncritically. On Twitter, Olly wrote, I know ppl who update their iOS regularly but who'll use the same views on politics, race, and gender they got 2nd hand 30 years ago.
  • What is Philosophy? and What has philosophy ever done for us? are two short videos by Massimo Pigliucci.
  • The Benefits of Philosophy and Reason is a 10-minute talk by Kenneth Allen Taylor at TEDx Stanford 2012.
  • Why Do Philosophy? is a podcast by The Partially Examined Life.
    (The name of the website and the YouTube channel are based on the statement attributed to Socrates that the unexamined life is not worth living. The quote continues to inspire articles, e.g. Simon Longstaff's article The unexamined life is not worth living, New Philosopher, 2 June 2013, and the article Socrates: An unexamined life is not worth living on the-philosophy.com, 3 May 2012.)
  • Nigel Warburton: Why studying Philosophy is so important? (42 minutes, 15 October 2015). This talk mentions benefits of studying philosophy (e.g. analytical skills and verbal reasoning) but that is not the most important benefit: the great value of philosophy is that it transforms the people who study it.
  • Philosophy saved me from poverty and drugs: that’s why I teach it to kids is an article by Andy West (The Guardian, 19 November 2015). Takeaway quote: I teach philosophy in schools because it allows young people to challenge authority and express themselves in a way that creates rather than destroys their life opportunities.
    Teaching philosophy to children? It's a great idea is a related article by Michelle Sowey (The Guardian, 20 November 2013) in which the author describes some of her own (positive!) experiences with teaching philosophy to primary school children and advocates the inclusion of philosophy in primary school curricula. Takeaway quote: Studying philosophy cultivates doubt without helplessness, and confidence without hubris. I’ve watched kids evolve to be more rational, sceptical and open-minded, and I’ve seen them interact in more fair-minded and collaborative ways. As one 10-year-old said, I’ve started to actually solve arguments and problems with philosophy. And it works better than violence or anything else.
  • Bertrand Russell: the everyday value of philosophy is an article by Clare Carlisle (The Guardian, 6 January 2014) that discusses the views on the purpose and importance of philosophy that Bertrand Russell expressed in his 1946 essay Philosophy for Laymen.
  • Working Your Degree is an article by Shelly K. Schwartz (CNN Money, 13 October 2000). It quotes philosophy professor Paul Boghossian, who says that a philosophy degree is very versatile because studying philosophy teaches students to capable of organiz[e] their thoughts and [express] them in a clear and rigorous form. The article also contains a wonderful quote from Alex Pozdnyakov, an immigrant from Russia, who explains why he wanted to study philosophy before tackling his final field of study: I have this strange phrase I use when people ask me why I chose philosophy. I tell them that I wanted to become a professional human being before becoming a professional. I thought philosophy would give me that and it did.
  • In Plato at the Googleplex: Why Philosophy Won't Go Away (Pantheon Books, 2014), Rebecca Newberger Goldstein argues that philosophy is still relevant today.
    The Atlantic published an interview with the author: Why Study Philosophy? 'To Challenge Your Own Point of View' (27 February 2014). The author also gave several talks based on the book, including a talk at Google (March 2014) and a talk at the British Humanist Association (20 October 2014). For a five-minute version of Goldstein's take on the relevance of philosophy, see this interview on KPBS (January 2015).
  • Leyla Hunn: Can I ever be successful as a Philosophy student?, Philosophy, University of Warwick (student blog), 24.11.2016.
  • The Daily Nous Value of Philosophy Pages (VPP) collect resources about the benefits of studying philosophy. See also Why Study Philosophy?.
  • How will learning about philosophy impact real-life? is a question on Philosophy Stack Exchange (a question and answer site about philosophy).
  • In the blog post Was Jesus a philosopher? (9 May 2010), the historian and philosopher of science John S. Wilkins discusses what philosophy is and why he thinks that Jesus was not a philosopher.
  • Mythos Philosophie - Brotlose Denker? is a short video (9 minutes) by the Bayerischer Rundfunk about the decreasing interest of students for philosophy in an age that focuses on utility.
  • Alex Voorhoeve: What's the Good of Ethics? is a five-minute interview by Conor Gearty. Alex Voorhoeve is Associate Professor at the Department of Philosophy, Logic and Scientific Method at the London School of Economics and Political Science (LSE).
  • Meiert, Jens Oliver: Why Philosophy Matters (31.10.2016):

Knowledge and Progress in Philosophy

Some people claim that philosophy makes no progress or even that it is dead (e.g. Stephen Hawking, which led to much debate). Below are some resources about progress and knowledge in philosophy.

  • Carrier, Richard: Is Philosophy Stupid? (on the Skepticon channel). This is a video recording of a presentation an the 6th Skepticon conference, which was held in Springfield, Missouri, in 2013. The slideshow, a summary of the slideshow and some links about criticism of philosophy are available on Richard Carrier's website.
  • Schnädelbach, Herbert: Was Philosophen wissen und was man von ihnen lernen kann. C. H. Beck, 2012. ISBN 978-3-406-63360-7.
    This book counters the idea that philosophy makes no progress by pointing out that introductory courses in philosophy have a lot of content in common, so there is an implicit consensus on what philosophers know. Schnädelbach tries to summarise this knowledge in a fairly accessible way. (However, the book is not a very easy read and assumes some knowledge of the history of philosophy.) Each chapter presents quite a lot of historical background to introduce something that philosophers “know”. In some cases, this knowledge comes down to “knowing” that certain ideas are not valid.
  • Gutting, Gary: What Philosophers Know: Case Studies in Recent Analytic Philosophy. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2009. ISBN 9780521672221.
    This book is similar to Schnädelbach's but focuses on contributions made by analytic philosophers such as W. V. O. Quine, Edmund Gettier, John Rawls, Thomas Kuhn and Richard Rorty.
  • Granger, Gilles-Gaston: Pour la connaissance philosophique. Odile Jacob, 1988. ISBN 9782738100238.
    This book discusses whether philosophy is a type of knowledge and whether there are philosophical truths.

Some philosophers take a different approach: instead of describing the achievements of philosophy, they argue that philosophy is still needed, in spite of all the progress that has been made in science.

  • Tallis, Raymond: Philosophy isn't dead yet, The Guardian, 27 May 2013. Raymond Tallis argues that science, especially physics, is in a mess and needs help from philosophy.
  • Baggini, Julian; Krauss, Lawrence: Philosophy v science: which can answer the big questions of life? The Observer, 09.11.2012.
  • Vallicella, Bill: When Philosophical Questions Grow Up Do They Leave Home? Some Bad Arguments of Lawrence Krauss Exposed, Maverick Philosopher (blog), 11.09.2012.
    Vallicella comments on the discussion between Julian Baggini and Lawrence Krauss, and points out how Krauss uses equivocation and other fallacious arguments (especially a confusion between facts and moral convictions; see also Hume's law). Vallicella has written many other blog posts criticsing Lawrence Krauss' bad philosophy.
  • Maudlin, Tim: Why Physics Needs Philosophy, The Nature of Reality (blog hosted by PBS), 23.04.2015.
    Tim Maudlin, author of Philosophy of Physics: Space and Time (2012), responds to Hawking's claim that philosophy is dead (expressed in The Grand Design, 2012). Maudlin writes that, The reigning attitude in physics has been “shut up and calculate”: solve the equations, and do not ask questions about what they mean.
  • Vallicella, Bill: Why Physics Needs Philosophy, Maverick Philosopher (blog), 26.04.2015.
    A comment on Tim Maudlin's article (see above).
  • Anthony, Andrew: Rebecca Newberger Goldstein: ‘Science is our best answer, but it takes a philosophical argument to prove that’, The Observer, 19.10.2014.
  • Darbonne, Dawn: Science needs philosophy, The Vermillion, 10.06.2014.
  • Papineau, David: Is philosophy simply harder than science?, TLS Online, 1 June 2017.
    Points out that philosophy tackles problems that are inherently difficult. Intractable problems also arise in the sciences, but scientists often prefer to ignore them.
  • Is Philosophy More Important Than Ever? (with Simon Critchley), Under The Skin with Russell Brand, 22.06.2017.
  • Wilson, Edward Osborne: Science, Not Philosophy, Will Explain the Meaning of Existence. In this 10-minute video, the biologist E. O. Wilson, author of The Meaning of Human Existence (Liveright / W. W. Norton, 2014), claims that philosophy is a declining and highly endangered academic species and that it has too many failed models of how the brain works (e.g. by Descartes and Kant). Philosophy is unable to explain the nature of humanity, so this task falls to science. Wilson mentions five scientific disciplines that are making progress in this area: evolutionary biology, paleontology & archeology (considered as a single discipline, but each looking at different timescales), brain science, artificial intelligence research, and robotics. These five disciplines, taken together, are beginning to tell us what the meaning of humanity is.
  • Loughlin, V. K.: Why philosophy is not dead: this is a blog by the Irish philosopher Victor Loughlin, who currently works at the University of Antwerp's Centre for Philosophical Psychology.
  • Blackford, Russell; Broderick, Damien, editors: Philosophy's Future: The Problem of Philosophical Progress. Wiley-Blackwell, 2017. (264 pages) ISBN 978-1-119-21008-5.
  • Healey, Richard: Why physicists need philosophy, OUP Blog, 09.12.2017.
    This blog post comments on the episode “Why are we here?” in a series of TV programs, in which Stephen Hawking claims that quantum theory explains why we exist. However, there is no consensus on how quantum theory should be interpreted.

Claims that philosophy does not produce knowledge or makes no progress can be found in the writings of both philosophers and scientsts:

  • Dietrich, Eric: There Is No Progress in Philosophy (PDF), Essays in Philosophy, 12.2 (July 2011: Philosophy's Future: Science or Something Else?).
    According to Dietrich, philosophy still deals with the same questions as 2500 years ago. Philosophy morphs and transforms to stay current: ideas and theories are reformulated in modern language, but that is all the progress there is. Philosophical theories that are definitely contradictory are equally plausible. Dietrich even argues that philosophy will never be able to solve any philosophical problems.
  • In the blog post What Kind of Knowledge Does Philosophy Discover? (30 August 2012), the biochemist Laurence A. Moran says that he and many others have reached the tentative conclusion that much of what passes for modern philosophy is a house of cards. It doesn't tell us anything. It doesn't produce knowledge, or truth. To those who claim that science cannot investigate the supernatural, he responds that scientists routinely apply the scientific way of knowing to questions about the supernatural. Does prayer work? Is their any evidence for life after death? and that [t]he scientific way of knowing is quite capable of discovering something that conflicts with or violates natural causes. So far, nothing has turned up but that doesn't mean we've never tried looking. Moran thinks that philosophers simply made up the claim that science cannot investigate the supernatural to protect both religion and philosophy from science.
  • David Chalmers: Why Isn't There More Progress in Philosophy? (YouTube, 55 minutes, 19.12.2013): Annual Lecture at Royal Institute of Philosphy, 2013.

Professional or Academic Philosophy

During the 19th century, philosophy became a discipline that was mainly practiced at universities. At the same time, it also became both much more technical and less relevant to the world outside academia. Some philosophers have voiced criticism against this trend.

  • Frodeman, Robert; Briggle, Adam: When Philosophy Lost Its Way, The Stone (Opinion Pages), The New York Times, 11.01.2016.
  • A Five-Point Manifesto for Anarcho-Philosophers and Other Real Philosophers, Against Professional Philosophy (APP), 02.07.2013.
    This text argues that academic philosophy is constantly dominated by a specific “core” and that anyone who wants to make a career as an academic philosopher must stick to the disciplines and basic ideas that constitute that core. (It is not clear whether this problem is equally severe outside North America.) The manifesto cites a paper by David Bourget and David J. Chalmers: What Do Philosophers Believe? (PDF) from 2013.
  • McIntyre, Lee: Making Philosophy Matter—or Else, The Chronicle of Higher Education, 11.12.2011.
    In 2011, budget cuts at the University of Nevada at Las Vegas threatened the existence of the university's philosophy department. The author argues that academic philosophers are to blame for the crisis in their profession. (The article has also been reposted on the APP blog.)
  • What is wrong with academic philosophy today?, Against the Logicians (blog), 24.08.2014.
  • More internal criticism of academic philosophy: Arvan on Graber, Against the Logicians (blog), 29.11.2014.
  • The Pseudo-Family from Hell: Against Philosophy Departments & For Philosophy Research Groups, Against Professional Philosophy (APP), 03.09.2015.

Epistemology

Since scientific knowledge is a type of knowledge, epistemology should be relevant to philosophy of science (even though both disciplines appear to evolve independently, according to Marko Beljac). One of the problems of epistemology that appears especially relevant to philosophy of science is the Gettier problem.

  • Gettier problem (Wikipedia).
  • Gettier Counterexamples (Philosophy Index).
  • Hetherington, Stephen: Gettier Problems (Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy).
  • The Analysis of Knowledge: The Gettier Problem (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy).
  • Beljac, Marko: The Gettier Problem in the Philosophy of Science, Analysans, 12.05.2016.

Organisations That Promote or Teach Philosophy

Organisations in Europe are listed first.

International Organisations

  • The Society for the Study of the History of Analytical Philosophy describes itself as an international organization aimed at promoting discussion in all areas of scholarship concerning the development of philosophical logic, philosophy of language, the philosophy of mind, metaphysics, the philosophy of science and epistemology. Membership is free. The society publishes the Journal for the History of Analytical Philosophy (JHAP).

United Kingdom

  • The Aristotelian Society was established in London in 1880. One of its activities is the organisation of philosophy talks that are open to the general public. These talks take place every other Monday at the Senate House of the University of London.
  • The Forum for European Philosophy at the LSE in London describes itself an an educational charity which organises and runs a full and varied programme of philosophy and interdisciplinary events in the UK. Almost all of their events are free to attend.
  • The Philosophy Foundation is an organisation that describes itself as an educational charity whose aim is to bring philosophy to schools and the wider community. They run philosophy sessions and workshops in schools, home education groups, businesses and communities. Their website also has a resources section with recommended reading, publications, lectures, “big questions” and more things to get you started.
  • The Institute of Philosophy at the School of Advanced Study was founded by the University of London in 2005. According to the description on its website, The Institute's aim is to promote and facilitate high quality research in philosophy, making it available to the widest possible audience both inside and outside the UK's academic community.

Germany

  • Die Anstifter is a voluntary association (eingetragener Verein) in Stuttgart, Germany. One of its activities is the Philosophisches Café with regular lectures and workshops on philosophy.
  • Forum für Philosophie is a GmbH in Frankfurt that organises conferences, courses and lectures on philosophy and also publishes books on philosophical topics.
  • Der philosophische Garten was founded by Matthias Christian Müller in Stuttgart in 2005. Matthias Müller organises lectures in Stuttgart and Berlin.
  • The Parmenides Foundation in Pullach near Munich does not teach philosophy but thinking. See its education page.

The Netherlands

  • The Internationale School voor Wijsbegeerte is a non-profit organisation in Leusden that organises courses and publishes books. They also have a Twitter account.

United States of America (USA)

  • PLATO: Philosophy Learning and Teaching Organization advocates and supports introducing philosophy to K-12 students through programs, resource-sharing and the development of a national network of those working in pre-college philosophy. Its website contains many resources, e.g. guidance and recommended books for teaching high school philosophy, guides to philosophy and lesson plans for teachers.
  • The Squire Family Foundation is an organisation that promotes the study of philosophy in elementary and secondary schools.
  • The American Philosophical Assocation (APA) promotes the discipline and profession of philosophy, both within the academy and in the public arena. The APA also has a Twitter account.

Australia and New Zealand

  • The Wheeler Centre in Melbourne hosts talks about many topics, and some of these talks were about philosophy. See also the Wheeler Centre's YouTube channel.

Bertrand Russell (1872 – 1970)

  • Russell, Betrand: The Basic Writings of Bertrand Russell. Routledge, 2009. (752 p.) ISBN 9780415472388.

It covers the full range of subjects that Russell wrote about, such as philosophy of language, logic, philosophy of mathematics, epistemology, history of philosophy, psychology, morals, politics and religion.

Intellectual Impostures

  • In 1997, Alan Sokal & Jean Bricmont published the book Impostures intellectuelles (English title Intellectual Impostures, 1998), in which they criticised the strange notions that some twentieth-century philosophers hold (or held) about science. See also the Sokal affair (Wikipedia).
  • Jean Bricmont: A Physicist Looks at Idealism and Relativism, a 90-minute lecture at the Université Catholique de Louvain in Belgium on 6 June 2014..
  • Richard Dawkins: Feminism Invading Science. This is an excerpt from a lecture that Dawkins gave at the Freedom from Religion convention in Portland, Oregon in 2012. (Here's the same excerpt as part of the full speech.) Dawkins quotes from his book review of Alan Sokal & Jean Bricmont's book Intellectual Impostures (1998). Sokal & Bricmont criticised Luce Irigaray's claim that E=mc2 is a “sexed equation” because it privileges the speed of light over other speeds that are vitally necessary to us.
