General Philosophy

Philosophy: Definition and Benefits

  • What is Philosophy? is a short video by Massimo Pigliucci.
  • What is Philosophy for? is a short video by The School of Life. The video explains that philosophy is relevant to us because we—“normal people”—don't ask big questions, are vulnerable to errors of common sense, are mentally confused, have muddled ideas about what makes us happy, and panic and lose perspective.
  • Why We Need Philosophy is a short video by Philosophy Tube. According to Olly, the creator of Philosophy Tube, we accept too many things uncritically. On Twitter, Olly wrote, I know ppl who update their iOS regularly but who'll use the same views on politics, race, and gender they got 2nd hand 30 years ago.
  • The Benefits of Philosophy and Reason is a 10-minute talk by Kenneth Allen Taylor at TEDx Stanford 2012.
  • Why Do Philosophy? is a podcast by The Partially Examined Life.
    (The name of the website and the YouTube channel are based on the statement attributed to Socrates that the unexamined life is not worth living. The quote continues to inspire articles, e.g. Simon Longstaff's article The unexamined life is not worth living, New Philosopher, 2 June 2013, and the article Socrates: An unexamined life is not worth living on the-philosophy.com, 3 May 2012.)
  • Nigel Warburton: Why studying Philosophy is so important? (42 minutes, 15 October 2015). This talk mentions benefits of studying philosophy (e.g. analytical skills and verbal reasoning) but that is not the most important benefit: the great value of philosophy is that it transforms the people who study it.
  • In the blog post Was Jesus a philosopher? (9 May 2010), the historian and philosopher of science John S. Wilkins discusses what philosophy is and why he thinks that Jesus was not a philosopher.
  • Mythos Philosophie - Brotlose Denker? is a short video (9 minutes) by the Bayerischer Rundfunk about the decreasing interest of students for philosophy in an age that focuses on utility.

Introductory Lectures and Videos

Also check the list of Free Online Philosophy Courses on Open Culture.

  • General Philosophy: an introduction taught by Peter Millican to first-year philosophy students at the University of Oxford. Links to slides, and audio and video downloads are available under a Creative Commons licence at the University of Oxford's podcasts website. The lectures are organised in eight parts. The first two parts give a historical overview from Aristotle to Hume. The other parts cover specific topics: the problem of induction, how we have knowledge of the world, the problem of perception, free will and moral responsibility, and finally the concept of personal identity. (There are 33 videos, each between 5 and 23 minutes long. The University of Oxford also has a lot of other content available under Creative Commons.)
  • Introduction to Philosophy: an introduction by Richard Brown. It starts with the question what philosophy is (first video) and then covers important philosophers from the presocratics to Kant. (13 videos, each between 33 and 70 minutes long).
  • Philosophical Techne is a channel by Mark Thorsby, a philosophy professor at Lone Star College in Texas. His playlists cover the History of Ancient Philosophy, an Introduction to Formal Logic, an Introduction to Philosophy and and Introduction to Social & Political Philosophy.
  • The Partially Examined Life is a YouTube channel with podcasts (with the same title) and other videos about philosophy. The podcasts and videos typically focus either on a specific philosopher or a specific topic.

  • Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature is a course by Tamar Gendler, who is Professor of Philosophy and Professor of Cognitive Science at Yale University. According to the course description, Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature pairs central texts from Western philosophical tradition (including works by Plato, Aristotle, Epictetus, Hobbes, Kant, Mill, Rawls, and Nozick) with recent findings in cognitive science and related fields. The course is structured around three intertwined sets of topics: Happiness and Flourishing; Morality and Justice; and Political Legitimacy and Social Structures. The course consists of 26 videos of 40 to 50 minutes. Course materials can be downloaded from the Open Yale Courses website. The course is also available on iTunes. (Yale Open Courses are mostly available under the CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 US licence.)
  • Philosophy is a course by Phil Fernandes, who is described as a Christian thinker at the Institute of Biblical Defense. The course consists of 15 videos that are between 15 and 90 minutes long.
  • Jules Evans: How philosophy can save your life, a talk at TEDx Breda, 2013. (Part of this talk is about cognitive behavioural therapy and how Albert Ellis was influenced by the Stoic philosopher Epictetus.)

Knowledge and Progress in Philosophy

Some people claim that philosophy makes no progress or even that it is dead (e.g. Stephen Hawking, which led to much debate). Below are some resources about progress and knowledge in philosophy.

  • Richard Carrier: Is Philosophy Stupid? (on the Skepticon channel). This is a video recording of a presentation an the 6th Skepticon conference, which was held in Springfield, Missouri, in 2013. The slideshow, a summary of the slideshow and some links about criticism of philosophy are available on Richard Carrier's website.
  • SchnÃ¤delbach, Herbert: Was Philosophen wissen und was man von ihnen lernen kann. C. H. Beck, 2012. ISBN 978-3-406-63360-7.
    This book counters the idea that philosophy makes no progress by pointing out that introductory courses in philosophy have a lot of content in common, so there is an implicit consensus on what philosophers know. SchnÃ¤delbach tries to summarise this knowledge in a fairly accessible way. (However, the book is not a very easy read and assumes some knowledge of the history of philosophy.) Each chapter presents quite a lot of historical background to introduce something that philosophers “know”. In some cases, this knowledge comes down to “knowing” that certain ideas are not valid.
  • Gutting, Gary: What Philosophers Know: Case Studies in Recent Analytic Philosophy. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2009. ISBN 9780521672221.
    This book is similar to SchnÃ¤delbach's but focuses on contributions made by analytic philosophers such as W. V. O. Quine, Edmund Gettier, John Rawls, Thomas Kuhn and Richard Rorty.
  • Granger, Gilles-Gaston: Pour la connaissance philosophique. Odile Jacob, 1988. ISBN 9782738100238.
    This book discusses whether philosophy is a type of knowledge and whether there are philosophical truths.

Claims that philosophy does not produce knowledge or makes no progress can be found in the writings of both philosophers and scientsts:

  • Dietrich, Eric: There Is No Progress in Philosophy (PDF), Essays in Philosophy, 12.2 (July 2011: Philosophy's Future: Science or Something Else?).
    According to Dietrich, philosophy still deals with the same questions as 2500 years ago. Philosophy morphs and transforms to stay current: ideas and theories are reformulated in modern language, but that is all the progress there is. Philosophical theories that are definitely contradictory are equally plausible. Dietrich even argues that philosophy will never be able to solve any philosophical problems.
  • In the blog post What Kind of Knowledge Does Philosophy Discover? (30 August 2012), the biochemist Laurence A. Moran says that he and many others have reached the tentative conclusion that much of what passes for modern philosophy is a house of cards. It doesn't tell us anything. It doesn't produce knowledge, or truth. To those who claim that science cannot investigate the supernatural, he responds that scientists routinely apply the scientific way of knowing to questions about the supernatural. Does prayer work? Is their any evidence for life after death? and that [t]he scientific way of knowing is quite capable of discovering something that conflicts with or violates natural causes. So far, nothing has turned up but that doesn't mean we've never tried looking. Moran thinks that philosophers simply made up the claim that science cannot investigate the supernatural to protect both religion and philosophy from science.

Copleston, Frederick: Greece and Rome (Volume 1), Medieval Philosophy (Volume 2), Late Medieval and Renaissance Philosophy (Volume 3), The Rationalists: Descartes to Leibniz (Volume 4), British Philosophy: Hobbes to Hume (Volume 5), The Enlightenment: Voltaire to Kant (Volume 6), 18th and 19th Century German Philosophy (Volume 7), Unitarianism to Early Analytic Philosophy (Volume 8), 19th and 20th Century French Philosophy (Volume 9), Russian Philosophy (Volume 10).

Frederick Copleston was a Jesuit priest and philospher. He published the first nine volumes of A History of Philosophy between between 1946 and 1974. The tenth volume on Russian philosophy was added in 1986.

The Humanities

Editorial note: this section will probably move to a separate page.

The English language makes a distinction between “science” and the “humanities”, thereby implying that the humanities are not true sciences. This opposition is often expressed as a distinction between hard and soft science or, as in C. P. Snow's famous book, The Two Cultures (1959). Other languages do not make this distinction and give the study of literature, linguistics, music etc. names that include a word for science. These disciplines are collectively known as Geisteswissenschaft(en) in German, humanidades in Spanish and alfawetenschappen in Dutch.

The humanities (which are usually considered to include philosophy) have come under pressure after sciences like physics became the model for scientific progress in general. A recent example is the closing of the Philosophy Department at Middlesex University. Some people have reacted to this by pointing out the value of the humanities to society. The British philosopher A. C. Grayling responded by founding the New College of the Humanities in London, which started offering tuition in 2012.

(Note: another distinction is between sciences, humanities and “social science” / sciences humaines et sociales (in French) / Sozialwissenschaften (in German). There are also defences of the social sciences similar to the defences of the humanities.)
