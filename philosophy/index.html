For starters, you can watch some YouTube videos (see below). Also check the list of Free Online Philosophy Courses on Open Culture.

General Philosophy

Philosophy: Definition and Benefits

  • What is Philosophy? is a short video by Massimo Pigliucci.
  • What is Philosophy for? is a short video by The School of Life. The video explains that philosophy is relevant to us because we—“normal people”—don't ask big questions, are vulnerable to errors of common sense, are mentally confused, have muddled ideas about what makes us happy, and panic and lose perspective.
  • Why Arts Graduates Are Under-Employed is another video by The School of Life. It discusses the value of arts and humanities, which includes philosophy.
  • The Benefits of Philosophy and Reason is a 10-minute talk by Kenneth Allen Taylor at TEDx Stanford 2012.
  • Why Do Philosophy? is a podcast by The Partially Examined Life. (The name of the website and the YouTube channel are based on the statement attributed to Socrates that the unexamined life is not worth living.)
  • Nigel Warburton: Why studying Philosophy is so important? (42 minutes, 15 October 2015). This talk mentions benefits of studying philosophy (e.g. analytical skills and verbal reasoning) but that is not the most important benefit: the great value of philosophy is that it transforms the people who study it.

Introductory Lectures and Videos

  • General Philosophy: an introduction taught by Peter Millican to first-year philosophy students at the University of Oxford. Links to slides, and audio and video downloads are available under a Creative Commons licence at the University of Oxford's podcasts website. (There are 33 videos, each between 5 and 23 minutes long. The University of Oxford also has a lot of other content available under Creative Commons.)
  • Introduction to Philosophy: an introduction by Richard Brown. It starts with the question what philosophy is (first video) and then covers important philosophers from the presocratics to Kant. (13 videos, each between 33 and 70 minutes long).
  • Philosophical Techne is a channel by Mark Thorsby, a philosophy professor at Lone Star College in Texas. His playlists cover the History of Ancient Philosophy, an Introduction to Formal Logic, an Introduction to Philosophy and and Introduction to Social & Political Philosophy.
  • The School of Life is a YouTube channel started by people who are fascinated by the sort of questions we're never taught enough about at school or college: How can relationships go well? What is meaningful work? How can love last? How can one find calm? What's gone wrong (and right) with capitalism? The makers of this channel also say, We love the humanities, especially philosophy, psychotherapy, literature and art - always going to them in search of ideas that are thought-provoking, useful and consoling. The channel started in September 2014; the videos are typically between 2 and 10 minutes long.
    The YouTube channel is only part of this initiative. See also www.theschooloflife.com and www.thebookoflife.org.
  • The Partially Examined Life is a YouTube channel with podcasts (with the same title) and other videos about philosophy. The podcasts and videos typically focus either on a specific philosopher or a specific topic.
    One of their playlists is The Partially Examined Life: PEL guests: this is a playlist of videos from various YouTube channels.
    Partially Examined Life Citizens is a related channel. The playlist PEL Episode Clips contains over 60 videos.
  • Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature is a course by Tamar Gendler, who is Professor of Philosophy and Professor of Cognitive Science at Yale University. According to the course description, Philosophy and the Science of Human Nature pairs central texts from Western philosophical tradition (including works by Plato, Aristotle, Epictetus, Hobbes, Kant, Mill, Rawls, and Nozick) with recent findings in cognitive science and related fields. The course is structured around three intertwined sets of topics: Happiness and Flourishing; Morality and Justice; and Political Legitimacy and Social Structures. The course consists of 26 videos of 40 to 50 minutes. Course materials can be downloaded from the Open Yale Courses website. The course is also available on iTunes. (Yale Open Courses are mostly available under the CC BY-NC-SA 3.0 US licence.)
  • Philosophy is a course by Phil Fernandes, who is described as a Christian thinker at the Institute of Biblical Defense. The course consists of 15 videos that are between 15 and 90 minutes long.
  • Jules Evans: How philosophy can save your life, a talk at TEDx Breda, 2013. (cognitive behavioral therapy and Epictetus)

Knowledge and Progress in Philosophy

Some people claim that philosophy makes no progress or even that it is dead (e.g. Stephen Hawking, which led to much debate). Below are some resources about progress and knowledge in philosophy.

  • Richard Carrier: Is Philosophy Stupid? (on the Skepticon channel). This is a video recording of a presentation an the 6th Skepticon conference, which was held in Springfield, Missouri, in 2013. The slideshow, a summary of the slideshow and some links about criticism of philosophy are available on Richard Carrier's website.
  • Schnädelbach, Herbert: Was Philosophen wissen und was man von ihnen lernen kann. C. H. Beck, 2012. ISBN 978-3-406-63360-7.
    This book counters the idea that philosophy makes no progress by pointing out that introductory courses in philosophy have a lot of content in common, so there is an implicit consensus on what philosophers know. Schnädelbach tries to summarise this knowledge in a fairly accessible way. (However, the book is not a very easy read and assumes some knowledge of the history of philosophy.) Each chapter presents quite a lot of historical background to introduce something that philosophers “know”. In some cases, this knowledge comes down to “knowing” that certain ideas are not valid.
  • Gutting, Gary: What Philosophers Know: Case Studies in Recent Analytic Philosophy. Cambridge: Cambridge University Press, 2009. ISBN 9780521672221.
    This book is similar to Schnädelbach's but focuses on contributions made by analytic philosophers such as W. V. O. Quine, Edmund Gettier, John Rawls, Thomas Kuhn and Richard Rorty.
  • Granger, Gilles-Gaston: Pour la connaissance philosophique. Odile Jacob, 1988. ISBN 9782738100238.
    This book discusses whether philosophy is a type of knowledge and whether there are philosophical truths.

Critical Thinking and Reasoning

  • Critical Reasoning for Beginners is a six-part course taught at the University of Oxford. Audio versions of the lectures and slideshows are available under a Creative Commons licence.
  • Critical Thinking, Logic, and Argumentation (ReasonIO) is a channel started by Gregory B. Sadler in October 2014. The videos focus on common fallacies such as straw man arguments, appeal to authority, wishful thinking and circular arguments. Each video is between 15 and 30 minutes long.
  • Logical and Critical Thinking, a free course on FutureLearn created by Tim Dare and Patrick Girard of the University of Auckland. The MOOC first started on 28 September 2015.
  • CriticalThinkingOrg is a channel by the Foundation for Critical Thinking, an American non-profit organisation that was founded by the Center for Critical Thinking in 1990. (The Center for Critical Thinking was founded in 1980 Dr. Richard Paul at Sonoma State University in Northern California.) The videos in the channel are organised in a number of playlists; most of these focus on how to teach and assess critical thinking.
  • Critical thinking: article on Wikipedia (in need of improvement).
  • Critical thinking, a section in Robert Todd Carroll's The Skeptic's Dictionary. It is an alphabetical list of cognitive biases, fallacies and related concepts. The bottom of the page has references to further reading, links to downloadable book chapters, etcetera.
  • List of cognitive biases on RationalWiki. According to the wiki's definition, Cognitive bias describes the inherent thinking errors that humans make in processing information. Many of these biases have articles of their own.
  • List of cognitive biases on Wikipedia. According to Wikipedia's definition, Cognitive biases are tendencies to think in certain ways that can lead to systematic deviations from a standard of rationality or good judgment, and are often studied in psychology and behavioral economics. Most of these biases have articles of their own.

History of Philosophy

A Little History of Philosophy (2012)

Warburton, Nigel: A Little History of Philosophy. New Haven & London: Yale University Press, 2012 (2011). (252 pages)
This very accessible book contains 40 chapters about great thinkers in the history of Western philosophy from Socrates to Peter Singer. The Yale University Press blog has an excerpt from the chapter on Kant and a short interview with the author.

YouTube playlists and videos about the history of philosophy:

  • History of Philosophy is a course by Arthur Holmes, who is described as a ”Christian scholar and philosopher“. The course focuses on Ancient Greek philosohy. It has 9 videos that are roughly between 40 and 60 minutes long. However, a longer, more complete playlist of the course, consisting of 81 videos, is available on the channel of Wheaton College. This version, A History of Philosophy, also covers philosophy after Antiquity up to the 20th century.

Specific Philosophers and Themes

  • In Our Time is a discussion series on BBC Radio 4. Some of the episodes are about philosophers; some of these have been collected on this channel. Each episode is roughly 42 minutes long.
    An archive of past episodes is also available on the BBC Radio 4 website.
  • Death is open course taught by Shelly Kagan of Yale University, who also published a book on the subject. The course has 26 videos that are between 35 and 50 minutes long. The course is also available on iTunes (and some YouTube users made copies of the course, e.g. Philosophy of Death).
  • Introduction to Political Philosophy is a open course taught by Steven B. Smith of Yale University. The course has 24 videos that are between 35 and 50 minutes long (roughly 45 minutes on average per video). Additional materials can be found on the course's homepage. The course is also available on iTunes.

Intellectual Impostures

  • In 1997, Alan Sokal & Jean Bricmont published the book Impostures intellectuelles (English title Intellectual Impostures, 1998), in which they criticised the strange notions that some twentieth-century philosophers hold (or held) about science. See also the Sokal affair (Wikipedia).
  • Jean Bricmont: A Physicist Looks at Idealism and Relativism, a 90-minute lecture.
  • Richard Dawkins: Feminism Invading Science. This is an excerpt from a lecture that Dawkins gave at the Freedom from Religion convention in Portland, Oregon in 2012. (Here's the same excerpt as part of the full speech.) Dawkins quotes from his book review of Alan Sokal & Jean Bricmont's book Intellectual Impostures (1998). Sokal & Bricmont criticised Luce Irigaray's claim that E=mc2 is a “sexed equation” because it privileges the speed of light over other speeds that are vitally necessary to us.
