Critical Thinking and Reasoning

Some of the resources below also cover the basics of logic. Resources that focus exclusively on logic are listed in a separate section.

  • Warburton, Nigel: Thinking from A to Z. Second edition. London: Routledge, 2000. ISBN: 0-415-22281-8 (2003 reprint). (150 p.)
    This book is an alphabetical compendium of techniques and fallacies that help sharpen the reader's critical thinking.
  • Kelley, David: The Art of Reasoning: An Introduction to Logic and Critical Thinking. 4th edition. W. W. Norton, 2013. (624 p.) ISBN 978-0-393-93078-8.
    This book covers topics such as reasoning (including argument analysis and fallacies), deductive logic and inductive logic (including statistical reasoning and probability). The book contains hundreds of excercices. (The accompanying website for the third edition is still online.)
  • Popova, Maria: The Baloney Detection Kit: Carl Sagan’s Rules for Bullshit-Busting and Critical Thinking, Brain Pickings, 03.01.2014.
    This article is based on the chapter “The Fine Art of Baloney Detection” from Carl Sagan's book The Demon­Haunted World: Science as a Candle in the Dark (1996).
    (See also Michael Shermer: Baloney Detection Kit, a video by the Richard Dawkins Foundation for Reason & Science.)
  • Critical Reasoning for Beginners is a six-part course taught in 2010 by Marianne Talbot at the University of Oxford's Department for Continuing Education. Audio versions of the lectures and slideshows are available under a Creative Commons licence.
    Marianne Talbot also wrote a book based on this course: Critical Reasoning: A Romp Through the Foothills of Logic (only available as e-book).
  • Critical Thinking, Logic, and Argumentation (ReasonIO) is a channel started by Gregory B. Sadler in October 2014. The videos focus on common fallacies such as straw man arguments, appeal to authority, wishful thinking and circular arguments. Each video is between 15 and 30 minutes long.
  • Logical and Critical Thinking, a free course on FutureLearn created by Tim Dare and Patrick Girard of the University of Auckland. The MOOC first started on 28 September 2015.
  • Three Elements of a Good Argument is the first part of a series of seven videos by Thad Botham about logic. The videos are between 10 and 75 minutes long.
  • CriticalThinkingOrg is a channel by the Foundation for Critical Thinking, an American non-profit organisation that was founded by the Center for Critical Thinking in 1990. (The Center for Critical Thinking was founded in 1980 Dr. Richard Paul at Sonoma State University in Northern California.) The videos in the channel are organised in a number of playlists; most of these focus on how to teach and assess critical thinking.
  • Critical Thinker Academy: online course with 18 hours of video.
  • Critical thinking: article on Wikipedia (in need of improvement).
  • Critical thinking, a section in Robert Todd Carroll's The Skeptic's Dictionary. It is an alphabetical list of cognitive biases, fallacies and related concepts. The bottom of the page has references to further reading, links to downloadable book chapters, etcetera.
  • ThinkWatson.com: a site by Pearson Education about critical thinking assessment and training.
  • DiCarlo, Christopher: How to Become a Really Good Pain in the Ass: A Critical Thinker's Guide to Asking the Right Questions. Prometheus Books, 2011. (398 p.) ISBN 978-1-61614-397-8.
  • Krauss, Lawrence M.: Why Schools Should Teach Skepticism Above Obedience, a short video by Big Think (on YouTube, 2 June 2017). See also Why Schools Should Teach Skepticism Above Obedience on the Big Think website.
  • The Skeptic's Field Guide.
  • Jones, Josh: Daniel Dennett Presents Seven Tools For Critical Thinking, Open Culture, 21 May 2013. Blog post based on Daniel Dennett's article in The Guardian (now removed), in which the author excerpted seven tools from his book Intuition Pumps and Other Tools for Thinking.
  • Peter Boghossian on the David Pakman Show: Faith vs Knowledge (Pt 1) (12 minutes), Defund "Radical Ideologues" in Gender Studies (Pt 2) (15 minutes), Does White Privilege Exist? (Pt 3) (12 minutes), What Happened to Critical Thinking? (Pt 3) (10 minutes). Uploaded on 1 August 2017.

Logic

  • Mun, Cecilea: Introduction to Logic. This is the first of a series of video lectures from an online introduction of philosophy. The chapters from the series correspond to the chapters in A Concise Introduction to Logic by Patrick Hurley (Cengage Learning, 2014; in spite of the title, this book has 736 pages).
  • Modal Logic is a playlist of 13 videos by Kane B. The videos vary in length from 4 to 18 minutes.
  • Hoyningen-Huene, Paul: Formale Logik. Eine philosophische Einführung is a series of 21 videos/lectures (taught in 2012) based on the book Formale Logik. Eine philosophische Einführung. Stuttgart: Reclam, 1998. ISBN 978-3-15-009692-5.
  • Hoyningen-Huene, Paul: Einführung in die Logik is a series of 22 videos based on the same course as the previous playlist, but uncut.
  • Logique et raisonnements by Exo7Math is part of a mathematics course. It consists of 19 videos that are each between 2 and 20 minutes long.

Cognitive Biases, Fallacies and Thinking Errors

  • List of cognitive biases on RationalWiki. According to the wiki's definition, Cognitive bias describes the inherent thinking errors that humans make in processing information. Many of these biases have articles of their own.
  • List of cognitive biases on Wikipedia. According to Wikipedia's definition, Cognitive biases are tendencies to think in certain ways that can lead to systematic deviations from a standard of rationality or good judgment, and are often studied in psychology and behavioral economics. Most of these biases have articles of their own.
  • The Fallacy Files: Taconomy of Logical Fallacies.
  • The Skeptic's Guide to the Universe: Logical Fallacies.
