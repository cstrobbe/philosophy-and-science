The Humanities

The English language makes a distinction between “science” and the “humanities”, thereby implying that the humanities are not true sciences. This opposition is often expressed as a distinction between hard and soft science or, as in C. P. Snow's famous lecture, The Two Cultures (1959). Other languages do not make this distinction and give the study of literature, linguistics, music etc. names that include a word for science. These disciplines are collectively known as Geisteswissenschaft(en) in German, humanidades in Spanish and alfawetenschappen in Dutch.

The humanities (which are usually considered to include philosophy) have come under pressure after sciences like physics became the model for scientific progress in general. Recent examples include the disappearance of language departments at State University of New York at Albany in 2010 and the closing of the Philosophy Department at Middlesex University. Some people have reacted to this by pointing out the value of the humanities to society. The British philosopher A. C. Grayling responded by founding the New College of the Humanities in London, which started offering tuition in 2012.

(Note: another distinction is between sciences, humanities and “social science” / sciences humaines et sociales (in French) / Sozialwissenschaften (in German). There are also defences of the social sciences similar to the defences of the humanities.)

The Two Cultures

“The Two Cultures” is the title of a famous lecture delivered in 1959 by the scientist and novelist C. P. Snow. The lecture was about the gulf between the sciences and the humanities. The British literary critic F. R. Leavis attacked Snow's statements in a lecture entitled Two Cultures? The Significance of C. P. Snow.

People sometimes ask why we need literary theory. Here is one answer (posted on a mailing list in 18 September 1995):

My short (perhaps equally naive) answer to a very complex question is that literary theory (l.t.) is a body of (more or less coherent) assumptions about what literature is, how and why to read and study it. These very general theoretical postulates can then be applied to/on different levels of the study of literature.

Looking at it in practical terms, literary theory more or less tries to theorize and systematize the conceptual frameworks shared by different (Western) literatures, like "poetic language", "poetry", "sonnet", "novel", "stream of consciousness", "metre", "character", "Modernism", "literary period", "literary history", "interpretation", etc.

Humanist Discussion Group, Vol. 9, No. 167.

Here is another point of view, from the blurb of Catherine Belsey's book Shakespeare in Theory and Practice (Edinburgh University Press, 2010):

Theory is valuable to the degree that it enables us to read differently: a nuanced approach shows that the most obvious interpretation is never the whole story.

The Grievance Studies Affair

The first list of links introduces the affair and presents resources created by the team of authors who wrote the fake academic papers.

  • Grievance Studies affair (Wikipedia).
    The Grievance Studies affair, also referred to as the "Sokal Squared" scandal (in reference to a similar 1996 hoax by Alan Sokal), was the project of a team of three authors (James A. Lindsay, Peter Boghossian, and Helen Pluckrose) to create bogus academic papers and submit them to academic journals in the areas of cultural, queer, race, gender, fat, and sexuality studies. The authors' intent was to expose problems in "grievance studies", a term they apply to a subcategory of these academic areas, in which they say a culture has developed in which only certain conclusions are allowed … and put social grievances ahead of objective truth.
  • Grievance Studies (on Peter Boghossian's website).
  • Lindsay, James A.; Boghossian, Peter; Pluckrose, Helen: Academic Grievance Studies and the Corruption of Scholarship, Areo, 02.10.2018. This essay, although hopefully accessible to everyone, is the most thorough breakdown of the study and written for those who are already somewhat familiar with the problems of ideologically-motivated scholarship, radical skepticism and cultural constructivism.
