The Humanities

The English language makes a distinction between “science” and the “humanities”, thereby implying that the humanities are not true sciences. This opposition is often expressed as a distinction between hard and soft science or, as in C. P. Snow's famous book, The Two Cultures (1959). Other languages do not make this distinction and give the study of literature, linguistics, music etc. names that include a word for science. These disciplines are collectively known as Geisteswissenschaft(en) in German, humanidades in Spanish and alfawetenschappen in Dutch.

The humanities (which are usually considered to include philosophy) have come under pressure after sciences like physics became the model for scientific progress in general. Recent examples include the disappearance of language departments at State University of New York at Albany in 2010 and the closing of the Philosophy Department at Middlesex University. Some people have reacted to this by pointing out the value of the humanities to society. The British philosopher A. C. Grayling responded by founding the New College of the Humanities in London, which started offering tuition in 2012.

(Note: another distinction is between sciences, humanities and “social science” / sciences humaines et sociales (in French) / Sozialwissenschaften (in German). There are also defences of the social sciences similar to the defences of the humanities.)

People sometimes ask why we need literary theory. Here is one answer (posted on a mailing list in 18 September 1995):

My short (perhaps equally naive) answer to a very complex question is that literary theory (l.t.) is a body of (more or less coherent) assumptions about what literature is, how and why to read and study it. These very general theoretical postulates can then be applied to/on different levels of the study of literature.

Looking at it in practical terms, literary theory more or less tries to theorize and systematize the conceptual frameworks shared by different (Western) literatures, like "poetic language", "poetry", "sonnet", "novel", "stream of consciousness", "metre", "character", "Modernism", "literary period", "literary history", "interpretation", etc.

Humanist Discussion Group, Vol. 9, No. 167.

Here is another point of view, from the blurb of Catherine Belsey's book Shakespeare in Theory and Practice (Edinburgh University Press, 2010):

Theory is valuable to the degree that it enables us to read differently: a nuanced approach shows that the most obvious interpretation is never the whole story.

Pop Culture: Game of Thrones

  • PBS NewsHour: What does a medieval lit scholar see in 'Game of Thrones?' (YouTube, 7 minutes): Brantley L. Bryant, associate professor of early and medieval British literature at Sonoma State University, discusses elements of medieval literature in the HBO TV show.
  • Mayes T.: The Real History Behind Game Of Thrones - Part 1 (YouTube, 18 minutes) and The Real History Behind Game Of Thrones - Part 2 (YouTube, 22 minute).
    Two important sources are the Wars of the Roses in 15th-century England and Maurice Druon's series of historival novels The Accursed Kings (Les Rois maudits). Valyrian steel was inspired by Damascus steel, whose method of production has been lost. The Red Wedding was inspired by the Black Dinner (1440) and the Massacre of Glencoe (1692). Cersei's walk of shame was inspired by Jane Shore's penance walk in the late 15th century. The Wall was inspired by Hadrian's Wall. The Knight's Watch was inspired by military orders such as the Knights Hospitaller and the Knights Templar. The Dothraki were inspired by nomadic people like the Huns and the Mongols. The Unsullied were inspired by slave armies such as the Mamluks and the Janissaries. The fighting pits of Meereen were inspired by the gladiator games in Ancient Rome.

  • Business Insider: Game of Thrones: Economics of Westeros (YouTube, 4 minutes).
  • It's Okay to Be Smart: The Science of Game of Thrones (YouTube, 8 minutes).

  • Rothman, Lily: The Physics of the Game of Thrones “Moon Door”, Time, 19.05.2014.

There may be more historical inspirations than are mentioned in the above sources. For example, the family name Martell is almost identical to Charles Martel's cognomen ("hammer"; "Martell" in German). Charles Martel fought important battles against Islamic forces, which has a kind of parallel to Dorne's resistance against the Targaryens, who also come from the east. Was Robert Baratheon's war hammer inspired by Charles Martel? Was the battle of Qohor, in which Dothraki cavalry charges again and again on the Unsullied inspired by the battle of Tours, in which the Islamic cavalry repeatedly charged against the Frankish infantry? (See this YouTube video about the Battle of Tours/Poitiers in 732.)
Tywin Lannister's first name looks almost identical to the name of the Welsh town Tywyn, but the pronunciation is quite different.
