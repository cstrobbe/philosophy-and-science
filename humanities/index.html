The Humanities

The English language makes a distinction between “science” and the “humanities”, thereby implying that the humanities are not true sciences. This opposition is often expressed as a distinction between hard and soft science or, as in C. P. Snow's famous book, The Two Cultures (1959). Other languages do not make this distinction and give the study of literature, linguistics, music etc. names that include a word for science. These disciplines are collectively known as Geisteswissenschaft(en) in German, humanidades in Spanish and alfawetenschappen in Dutch.

The humanities (which are usually considered to include philosophy) have come under pressure after sciences like physics became the model for scientific progress in general. Recent examples include the disappearance of language departments at State University of New York at Albany in 2010 and the closing of the Philosophy Department at Middlesex University. Some people have reacted to this by pointing out the value of the humanities to society. The British philosopher A. C. Grayling responded by founding the New College of the Humanities in London, which started offering tuition in 2012.

(Note: another distinction is between sciences, humanities and “social science” / sciences humaines et sociales (in French) / Sozialwissenschaften (in German). There are also defences of the social sciences similar to the defences of the humanities.)

A Year of Reading the World

In 2011, Ann Morgan, who had grown up with books by British and North-American authors, set herself the task to read a book from each country on the planet. For some countries, it was hard to find books that had been translated into English, but Morgan received help from many volunteers.
