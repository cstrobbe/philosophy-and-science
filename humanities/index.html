The Humanities

The English language makes a distinction between “science” and the “humanities”, thereby implying that the humanities are not true sciences. This opposition is often expressed as a distinction between hard and soft science or, as in C. P. Snow's famous lecture, The Two Cultures (1959). Other languages do not make this distinction and give the study of literature, linguistics, music etc. names that include a word for science. These disciplines are collectively known as Geisteswissenschaft(en) in German, humanidades in Spanish and alfawetenschappen in Dutch.

The humanities (which are usually considered to include philosophy) have come under pressure after sciences like physics became the model for scientific progress in general. Recent examples include the disappearance of language departments at State University of New York at Albany in 2010 and the closing of the Philosophy Department at Middlesex University. Some people have reacted to this by pointing out the value of the humanities to society. The British philosopher A. C. Grayling responded by founding the New College of the Humanities in London, which started offering tuition in 2012.

(Note: another distinction is between sciences, humanities and “social science” / sciences humaines et sociales (in French) / Sozialwissenschaften (in German). There are also defences of the social sciences similar to the defences of the humanities.)

Dead Poets Society and the Humanities

The film Dead Poets Society sparked comments about the humanites and how to teach them.

  • Dettmar, Kevin J. H.: Dead Poets Society Is a Terrible Defense of the Humanities, The Atlantic, 19.02.2014.
    In one of the scenes that made the film Dead Poets Society famous, English teacher John Keating lets his students rip out the introduction the introducion to their poetry book because he wants them to think for themselves. What he really does, however, is replacing critical reading with “appreciation” and instead of teaching his students to look at things in a different way, he merely teaches his own “different way”. According to Dettmar, Keating represents what he calls “sentimental humanities”: humanities content stripped of all humanities methodology and rigor; this reductive view is damaging to the humanities. (Dettmar also points out that Keating misreads the poems he teaches.)
    Dettmar challenges the misconception that humanities doesnt't train thinkers; instead: To read literature well is to be challenged, and to emerge changed. He links to examples of neuroscience that also point in this direction. (See This is your brain on Jane Austen, and Stanford researchers are taking notes, Stanford News, 07.09.2012, and Reading a Novel Changes Your Brain The Atlantic, January 2014.) On the other hand, the humanities turned to theory in the 1970 and 1980s, a development that was accompanied by politicization. This politicization led to a backlash; some people don't want the humanities to be too intellectual.
  • Waurechen, Sarah: Picking Apart the Vision of Humanistic Teaching and Learning in Dead Poet’s Society, Ph.D.s and Pedagogy, 17.03.2014.
    Sarah Waurechen partly agrees with Dettmar's criticism but adds that Dettmar is misreading the film as badly as Keating misreads all that poetry. She thinks the film critiques the carefree, youthful romance with a smattering of dead poets. Early in the film, Keating questions the limitations of freedom: But only in their dreams can men be truly free. ‘Twas always thus, and always thus will be. However, she admits that Keating's way of encouraging non-conformity made her uncomfortable. The students should at least learn to understand what they rebel against and that Keating does not help them: They must become the self-aware and critical beings that Keating briefly gestures towards and then ignores.
    In the film's final scene, half of the students remain seated: the choice to stand becomes more individual and more critical. For Waurechen, the movie is indeed an excellent defense of the Humanities, if you can watch it with enough of a critical eye to see that it’s not Keating who embodies true learning.
  • Berkson, David: Dead Poets Society: The Pedagogy of Sentimentality and Uncritical Thinking, The Pigeon Press, 03.11.2017.
    According to the author, Dead Poets Society corrupts the pedagogical principles it smugly purports to champion. The film protrays John Keating as a phenomenal and progressive teacher, whereas in reality he is a bad teacher. First, there isn't much direct interplay between teacher and students except for the “barbaric yawp” scene; Keating is too much of a stand-up comedian. Second, Keating has no interest in critical thinking. When he instructs his students to rip the introduction out of their poetry textbook, the message is analysis leads to conformity, and conformity is bad. He never asks what his students think of the introduction, in spite of saying, Now when you read, don’t just consider what the author thinks, consider what you think. Third, Keating is an egomaniac: he tells his students to address him as “O Captain! my Captain!” instead of “Mr. Keating”, implicitly comparing himself to Abraham Lincoln. In the film's final scene, the students clearly express their allegiance to him. Keating is also a coward; when he thinks his job is at stake, he reprimands Charlie Dalton for putting his “carpe diem” and his anti-authoritarian views into practice. Neil Perry doesn't get any support from him, either. Finally, the film eroticises Neil Perry's suicide. Berkson concludes that Dead Poets Society a morally bankrupt piece of emotional pornography, profoundly smug, deeply irresponsible, and ill-deserving of its many accolades.
  • Porter, Lavelle: Against the Sentimental Humanities: Reading Wit, The Over-Educated Negro, 13.02.2015.
    This blog post is not primarily about Dead Poets Society. Porter writes: Dead Poets Society is about stimulating an enthusiasm for reading and learning in high school kids, whereas Wit is about sharpening and refining those sensibilities into rigorous scholarship at the college level. Perhaps. Wit is a one-act play by Margaret Edson that was adapted into television film.
  • Heon, Elizabeth: Dead Poets Society: Don’t rip out the intro!, Rose Scholars Fall 2017 (blog), 26.10.2017.
    This author also finds the film weirdly anti-intellectual and adds that given our current political climate, it’s not super fun to watch a high school teacher essentially tell his students that the truth is whatever you feel is true.

The Two Cultures

“The Two Cultures” is the title of a famous lecture delivered in 1959 by the scientist and novelist C. P. Snow. The lecture was about the gulf between the sciences and the humanities. The British literary critic F. R. Leavis attacked Snow's statements in a lecture entitled Two Cultures? The Significance of C. P. Snow.

People sometimes ask why we need literary theory. Here is one answer (posted on a mailing list in 18 September 1995):

My short (perhaps equally naive) answer to a very complex question is that literary theory (l.t.) is a body of (more or less coherent) assumptions about what literature is, how and why to read and study it. These very general theoretical postulates can then be applied to/on different levels of the study of literature.

Looking at it in practical terms, literary theory more or less tries to theorize and systematize the conceptual frameworks shared by different (Western) literatures, like "poetic language", "poetry", "sonnet", "novel", "stream of consciousness", "metre", "character", "Modernism", "literary period", "literary history", "interpretation", etc.

Humanist Discussion Group, Vol. 9, No. 167.

Here is another point of view, from the blurb of Catherine Belsey's book Shakespeare in Theory and Practice (Edinburgh University Press, 2010):

Theory is valuable to the degree that it enables us to read differently: a nuanced approach shows that the most obvious interpretation is never the whole story.

The Grievance Studies Affair

The first list of links introduces the affair and presents resources created by the team of authors who wrote the fake academic papers.

  • Grievance Studies affair (Wikipedia).
    The Grievance Studies affair, also referred to as the "Sokal Squared" scandal (in reference to a similar 1996 hoax by Alan Sokal), was the project of a team of three authors (James A. Lindsay, Peter Boghossian, and Helen Pluckrose) to create bogus academic papers and submit them to academic journals in the areas of cultural, queer, race, gender, fat, and sexuality studies. The authors' intent was to expose problems in "grievance studies", a term they apply to a subcategory of these academic areas, in which they say a culture has developed in which only certain conclusions are allowed … and put social grievances ahead of objective truth.
  • Grievance Studies (on Peter Boghossian's website).
  • Lindsay, James A.; Boghossian, Peter; Pluckrose, Helen: Academic Grievance Studies and the Corruption of Scholarship, Areo, 02.10.2018. This essay, although hopefully accessible to everyone, is the most thorough breakdown of the study and written for those who are already somewhat familiar with the problems of ideologically-motivated scholarship, radical skepticism and cultural constructivism.

Organisations and initiative promoting the humanities:

  • The School of Life is a YouTube channel started by people who are fascinated by the sort of questions we're never taught enough about at school or college: How can relationships go well? What is meaningful work? How can love last? How can one find calm? What's gone wrong (and right) with capitalism? The makers of this channel also say, We love the humanities, especially philosophy, psychotherapy, literature and art - always going to them in search of ideas that are thought-provoking, useful and consoling. The channel started in September 2014; the videos are typically between 2 and 10 minutes long.
    The YouTube channel is only part of this initiative. See also www.theschooloflife.com and www.thebookoflife.org.
  • Académie Cicéron. The Académie Cicéron was founded in November 2019 in order to promote general cultural knowledge. The initiative came as a reaction against the elimination of the topic of general culture from certain entrance exams, especially at Sciences Po. See also Académie Cicéron on Twitter.
