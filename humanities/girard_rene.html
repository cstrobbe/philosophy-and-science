Introduction

René Girard (1923–2015) was a French literary critic and philosopher whose work is difficult to categorise. In addition to literature and philosophy, his work is also relevant philosophical anthropology, psychology and economics (although he did not publish in all these areas). His work is based on the analysis of literary and religious texts, not on actual research in psychology or anthropology (i.e. no psychological experiments, ethnohistoric research or ethnographic research).

After writing a PhD dissertation in history, he moved from France to the United States, where his first journal articles focused on French literature. In his first book, Mensonge romantique et vérité romanesque (1961), he first formulated his theory about mimetic desire, based on an analysis of novels by Cervantes, Flaubert, Stendhal, Proust and Dostoyevski. His second major work, La Violence et le sacré (1972) is the result of thinking through the consequences of mimesis for early human societies. This led to the formulation of his theory about the role of the scapegoat mechanism in the emergence of human culture and religion. His 1978 book Des choses cachées depuis la fondation du monde (1978) continued his work in this direction and added his theory that the Bible reveals and denounces the scapegoat mechanism. Several other books followed, including La route antique des hommes pervers (1985), which focuses on the book of Job, and A Theater of Envy: William Shakespeare (1991), in which he shows that Shakespeare was aware of the mechanisms of mimesis and scapegoating.

Literary Theory

Much of René Girard's work consists of analysis of literary texts, not so much for the sake of understanding literature itself, but in order to gain a better understanding of social behaviour.

In literary theory, René Girard is sometimes categorised as a structuralist. In the late 1960s, Girard contributed to the volume The Structuralist Controversy: The Languages of Criticism and the Sciences of Man (edited by Richard Macksey & Eugenio Donato; Johns Hopkins University Press, 1967). This book was the outcome of a conference, where Girard presented a paper entitled Tiresias and the Critic. Later, Girard abandoned the idea that he should join forces with structuralist literary theory. In spite of this, some recent publications still consider his work as an example of the structuralist approach, e.g. Shakespeare & Literary Theory by Jonathan Gil Harris (Oxford University Press, 2010).

Websites

  • The Colloquium on Violence & Religion (COV&R): this is an international association founded in 1990 that publishes the yearly journal Contagion and a bulletin (for members), and that organises a yearly conference devoted to the ramifications of Girard's mimetic theory for theology, philosophical anthropology, psychology and literary criticism.
  • Imitatio is a project of the Thiel Foundation that furthers research and the application of Girard's mimetic theory across the social sciences. The Imitatio website contains useful resources such as Resources (on Mimetic Theory) and A Course with Professor Girard (by Andrew Feenberg).
    Peter Thiel was influenced by the mimetic theory (see this interview on YouTube). See also Imitatio's YouTube channel.
  • The Raven Foundation (established in 2007) is committed to making religion reasonable, violence unthinkable and peace a possibility by spreading awareness of the transformative power of mimetic theory. See also the Raven Foundation's YouTube channel.
  • Association Recherches Mimétiques (established in 2006) is a French organisation that aims to structure and make widely known the research inspired by René Girard's mimetic theory. It is the French branch of Imitatio. The organisation also has a YouTube channel.
  • Girard Studiekring: the Dutch Girard Society, which started meeting in the 1980s. Its entire website is in Dutch.
  • Site Violence et Sacré, French website by Jean-Paul Kornobis. An English version of the home page is also available, but the rest of the site has not been translated.
  • René Girard, French website by Philippe Cottet.
  • Anthropoetics: The Journal of Generative Anthropology is an Open Access journal that took its starting point from Girard's theory about the origin of human culture. Eric Gans, who started the journal, also wrote A Brief Introduction to Generative Anthropology.
  • Andrade, Gabriel: René Girard (1923—2015): entry in the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy.
  • René Girard Archives: YouTube channels with videos of conference talks, lectures and debates with René Girard, usually in French.
  • Morrissey, Christopher S.: The René Girard Bibliography.

Publications by René Girard

René Girard published many books. Most of them are monographs; some are collections of journal articles.

  • Girard, René: Mensonge romantique et vérité romanesque. Grasset, 1961 (1977). (320 pages) ISBN 9782246004073.
    Girard's first book analyses novels by Cervantes, Flaubert, Stendahl and Dostoyevsky, and formulates his theory of mimetic desire. The book was reprinted in the series Les Cahiers Rouges in 2001.

  • Girard, René: Dostoïevski : du double à l’unité. Gérard Monfort, 1963.
    An analysis of several of Dostoyevsky's novels, starting with Notes from Underground. This essay was later reprinted in Critique dans un souterrain (see below).
  • Girard, René: La Violence et le sacré. Grasset, 1972. (456 pages) ISBN 9782246000518.
    In his third book, Girard looks for mimesis in myths and older literary texts (especially Greek tragedy) and describes how mimesis leads to scapegoating and the origin of human culture and religion.

Obituaries and Commemorations

René Girard died in Stanford in the USA on 4 November 2015. Below is a list of obituaries and commemorations. (This web page was created in response to René Girard's death, which is why this section takes up so much space here.)

  • Haven, Cynthia: Stanford professor and eminent French theorist René Girard, member of the Académie Française, dies at 91, Stanford Report, 04.11.2015.
    This is the original announcement of René Girard's death. Cynhia Haven quotes the following characterisation by Professor Robert Pogue Harrison: The best analogy for what René represents in anthropology and sociology is Heinrich Schliemann, who took Homer under his arm and discovered Troy. René had the same blind faith that the literary text held the literal truth. Like Schliemann, his major discovery was excoriated for using the wrong methods. Academic disciplines are more committed to methodology than truth.
  • Chantre, Benoît: René Girard est décédé le 4 novembre 2015, Association Recherches Mimétiques.

  • Aissaoui, Mohammed: Mort de René Girard : les réactions de François Hollande et du monde de la culture, Le Figaro, 05.11.2015.
    Reactions from the French president François Hollande, Jacques Attali, the French minister of culture Fleur Pellerin and a few others.
  • Tincq, Henri: René Girard, l'homme qui nous aidait à penser la violence et le sacré, Slate.fr, 05.11.2016.
  • Birnbaum, Jean: Mort de René Girard, anthropologue et théoricien du « désir mimétique », Le Monde, 05.11.2015.
    This article contains a nice short biography that starts with Girard's childhood in Avignon. It also explains how Girard moved from history to literature (after his arrival in the USA) and later from literature to anthropology. It describes how Girard came to see himself as a Christian apologetic and the New Testament as a source of human science. Girard also claimed that his theories were based on “common sense”.
  • Guillebaud, Jean-Claude: La mort de René Girard, penseur de la violence, Le Nouvel Observateur, 13.08.2010, updated 05.11.2015.
    According to Guillebaud, Girard's work is still not well known to the general public in France. After reading Girard, one can no longer look at human history in the same way as before.
  • Guillebaud, Jean-Claude: Quand René Girard s'expliquait sur les ruses du désir, Le Nouvel Observateur, 05.11.2015.
    Reprint of an interview from 2004, at the occastion of the publication of     Les Origines de la culture. Two quotes from the interview:
    [L]es méthodes objectives telles que l'analyse sociologique ou la psychanalyse restent grossières, mensongères même, dans la mesure où le mimétisme toujours individuel de nos désirs et de leurs conflits leur échappe. Les méthodes faussement objectives ne tiennent aucun compte de l'influence qu'exerce sur chacun de nous notre propre expérience, notre existence concrète.
    Je définis donc comme «conversion» le pouvoir de se détacher suffisamment de soi pour dévoiler ce qu'il y a de plus secrètement public en chacun de nous, le modèle, collectif ou individuel, qui domine notre désir.
    Je crois que les intellectuels sont même fréquemment moins clairvoyants que la foule car leur désir de se distinguer les pousse à se précipiter vers l'absurdité à la mode alors que l'individu moyen devine le plus souvent, mais pas toujours, que la mode déteste le bon sens.
  • Lapaque, Sebastien: Mort de l'académicien René Girard, Le Figaro, 05.11.2015.
    Contains the following quote from Girard on the importance of reading the Gospels and great works of Western literature: Je suis personnellement convaincu que les écrivains occidentaux majeurs, qu'ils soient ou non chrétiens, des tragiques grecs à Dante, de Shakespeare à Cervantès ou Pascal et jusqu'aux grands romanciers et poètes de notre époque, sont plus pertinents pour comprendre le drame de la modernité que tous nos philosophes et tous nos savants.
    Le Figaro also reprinted the article that Girard had written in defence of Mel Gibson's film The Passion of the Christ: La Passion du Christ vue par René Girard.

    Bowyer writes that Girard's influence is still in its infancy, even though he managed to create a framework which has been used to integrate anthropology, sociology, economics, religious studies, and to some degree economics, into one set of basic ideas. The main part of the article summarises the mimetic theory.
    Bowyer concludes with a few comments about two “applied Girardians”. While academic Girardians tend to the political left, the billionaire Peter Thiel, who studied with Girard, leans towards libertarianism. Thiel's book Zero to One is drenched in Girardian insights. Bowyer also points out: Left-Girardism shows just how insidious mimesis and scapegoat mechanisms are — even Girardians fall into them. The ‘top 1%’ is a particularly tempting target of the scapegoat mechanism. Reverend Richard Bledsoe, a pastor in Boulder, Colorado, finds the mimetic theory very practical and has written a manual on the spiritual and pastoral uses of Girard.
    In his last interview (with Peter Robinson in 2009), Girard had said that in order to avoid global violence, people should simply act like Christians.
