In der Naturwissenschaft gibt es gleichwie in der Kunst und im Leben keine andere Naturtreue als die Kulturtreue. (In natural science, as in art and in life, being true to nature is only possible by being true to culture.) Ludwik Fleck, Entstehung und Entwicklung einer wissenschaftlichen Tatsache. Frankfurt am Main: Suhrkamp, 1980: 48.

Summary

Ludwik Fleck (1896-1961) was a Polish serologist and microbiologist who worked, among other things, on vaccines for typhus. In his spare time, he also published articles and a monograph on philosophy of science and sociology of science. He coined the concepts “thought style” (Denkstil) and “thought collective” (Denkkollektiv) to describe how scientific ideas and discoveries are also determined by their social and historical context. He published his main work, Entstehung und Entwicklung einer wissenschaftlichen Tatsache, in 1935, just one year after the publication of Logik der Forschung by Karl Raimund Popper. Both Popper and Fleck took issue with the logical positivism advocated by the Vienna Circle, but unlike Popper's book, Fleck's work was overlooked by most philosophers of science.
After the Nazis captured his hometown Lvov in 1941, he first lived in a ghetto and later in Buchenwald. Both in the ghetto and later in Buchenwald, he worked on a typhus vaccine (in addition to other medical work). After the war, Fleck continued his work in microbiology and immunology. He emigrated to Israel in 1956, where he died in 1961.
His work influenced Thomas Kuhn's The Structure of Scientific Revolutions, which was published just one year after Fleck's death. The Fleck renaissance that started in the 1970s initially gave rise to studies on Fleck as a precursor of Kuhn, but Fleck's theory is now studies in its own right and has been applied to the study of as diverse as aesthetics, (the history of) quantum mechanics, science pedagogy and visualisation in medicine (Fleck, 2011: 13-14). There were at least five Fleck conferences between 2005 and 2011.

Fleck's work in philosophy of sociology of science is significant in several respects:

  • Fleck was the first philosopher to look at science from a sociological point of view; this sociological approach did not gain popularity until the publication of Thomas Kuhn's The Structure of Scientific Revolutions in 1961.
  • Fleck was the first philosopher of science who did not use physics as the starting point for his theory, unlike, for example, Popper and Kuhn. Physics remained the dominant “model” of science until long after Fleck's death. As Fleck wrote in a letter to Moritz Schlick in 1933, he found physics, astronomy and chemistry inappropriate for the study of the genesis of scientific knowledge because many discoveries lie so far back in time that it is no longer possible to study them without the interference from ideas that are part of school education or history books (Fleck, 2011: 13).

Biography

The first researchers to collect biographical information on Ludwik Fleck were W. Baldamus (1976, 1977) and Thomas Schnelle (1982). The biography in 1983 edition of Entstehung und Entwicklung einer wissenschaftlichen Tatsache closely follows the biography by Schnelle (1982). Grzybowski & Ciesielska (2014) published additional information based on materials from the Archives of the Auschwitz-Birkenau State Museum. Arthur Allen's book (2014) also contains some biographical information, although much of it is scattered throughout the book. The following biography is mainly based on Thomas Schnelle's disseration (1982).

Before World War II

Ludwik Fleck was born 11 July 1896 in Lvov. At the time, the city was known as Lemberg and was the capital of the Kingdom of Galicia and Lodomeria, a crown land of the Habsburg Monarchy from 1804 to 1918. The Austro-Hungarian Empire allowed its territories a certain degree of cultural autonomy, so Lemberg had Polish schools and its university was allowed to teach on Polish from 1879 onwards. (See also Galician autonomy on Wikipedia.) Thanks to this cultural tolerance, German-language culture met with a lot of interest.

Ludwik Fleck went to a Polish gymnasium but also learnt perfect German (just like his two sisters). In 1914, Fleck started studying medicine at John II Casimir University (or Jan Kazimierz University), but his studies were soon interrupted by the outbreak of the First World War. Nevertheless, he received his medical degree in 1918.

In 1920, Fleck became an assistant of the typhus expert Rudolf Weigl in Przemyśl. When Weigl became a professor in the Department of General Biology at Jan Kazimierz University, Fleck followed him there and worked at the university until 1923. While working as Weigl's assistant, Fleck discovered and developed a skin test for the diagnosis of typhus, the “exanthin reaction” (Fleck, 1923).

Typhus is a disease caused by several strains of Rickettsia bacteria. The bacteria are transmitted between humans (or to humans) by parasites such as fleas and ticks. The form of typhus studied by Weigl and Fleck was epidemic typhus, which is caused by the bacteria species Rickettsia prowazekii and is transmitted by body lice (Pediculus humanus humanus). The infection is not caused by the louse bites themselves: the bites cause itching, so the victim scratches the area of the bite and thereby rubs the lice faeces into the bite, thereby causing the infection. Typhus caused many victims on the eastern front line during both World Wars: especially in POW camps (World War I), in concentration camps and the Battle of Stalingrad (World War II).

The French bacteriologist Charles Nicolle discovered that body lice were the vector of the disease in 1909 (Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, 1928) but was not able to develope a practical vaccine. The first research to develop an effective vaccine was the Polisch biologist Rudolf Weigl, in the early 1930s. The vaccine was hard to produce on a large scale because it involved feeding infected lice on human volunteers and crushing the lice intestines into a vaccine paste. In the late 1930s, the American bacteriologist Herald R. Cox developed a safer production technique after he discovered that he could grow Rickettsia in fertile egg membranes.

Typhus should not be confused with typhoid fever. The two diseases have similar names because doctors observed similar symptoms before it was possible to identify the vectors and agents of both diseases. While epidemic typhus is caused by the Rickettsia prowazekii, which is carried by body lice, typhoid fever is caused by Salmonella typhi bacteria that are ingested when eating or drinking foor or water that is contaminated with the faeces of an infected person. The names of the two diseases are also often confused in translations, especially when translating between Germanic languages. Typhus is called Fleckfieber in German, typhus (exanthématique) in French, tifus (exantemático epidémico) in Spanish and vlektyfus in Dutch. Typhoid fever is called Typhus in German, fièvre typhoïde in French, fiebre tifoidea in Spanish and buiktyfus in Dutch.

Between 1923 and 1925, Fleck worked at the department for internal medicine at Lvov's general hospital; between 1925 and 1927 he was the head of the bacteriological lab of the department for dermatology and venereology in the same hospital. From 1923 until 1939, Fleck could not gain a new university position; this was probably due to anti-Jewisch tensions. In 1923, he had started a private bacteriological lab, where he continued his own research.

In 1927, Fleck spent six weeks in Vienna, where he worked at the State Serotherapeutic Institute. (At that time, the Vienna Circle was in a non-public phase.) After his return to Lvov in 1928, Fleck became head of the bacteriological lab of the local health maintenance organization. From 1935 on, he worked exclusively in his private laboratory. From the late 1920s to the late 1930s, Fleck's research focused on the diagnosis of syphilis, tuberculosis (Lupus erythematodes) and pemphigus. He also developed an interest in more general serological questions, e.g. the clumping of leukocytes (for which he worked with the mathematician Hugo Steinhaus).

In the 1920s he also started publishing articles on philosophy of science. Fleck had already studied philosophy at university, and he continued reading philosophy, sociology and history of science after receiving his medical degree. His reading in this areas was apparently not very systematic. He worked on Entstehung und Entwicklung einer wissenschaftlichen Tatsache between 1931 and 1934.
Fleck was very selective in the people with whom he discussed his work. One of the people with whom Fleck had regular discussions was the psychiatrist and philosopher Jakub or Jakób Frostig.

Lvov had a rich intellectual life and its university had many distinguished researchers: the mathematicians Hugo Steinhaus and Stefan Banach, the biologist Stanisław Kulczyński, the biochemist Jakub / Yacov Parnas, the microbiologist Rudolf Weigl, the pediatrician Franciszek Groër, the economists Leopold Caro and Stanisław Grabski, and the anthropologist, statistician and linguist Jan Czekanowski.
In Lvov, there was less interest in the philosophy of medicine than in Warsaw. At least Witold Ziembicki had an interest in this topic. Ziembicki was head of the department for internal medicine at the state hospital in Lvov and held lecture on the history of medicine at the university.
Scientists and researchers in Lvov also met in interdisciplinary discussion groups.

Fleck was also in contact with the philosophers from the Lvov-Warsaw School, which had been established by Kazimierz Twardowski (1866-1938). Its most well-known members were Kazimierz Ajdukiewicz, Tadeusz Kotarbiński, Władysław Tatarkiewicz, Jan Łukasiewicz and Alfred Tarski. Twardowski's lectures were attended by students from all disciplines and by guests from other universities; the university's biggest lecture hall was too small so the lectures has to be held in the city's biggest concert hall.
The other chair in philosophy was held by Mścisław Wartenberg, who specialised in Kant and Neo-Kantianism. He was not nearly as influential as Twardowski.
Two other important philosophers who stood outside Twardowski's sphere of influence were Leon Chwistek and Roman Ingarden. Fleck was probably in close contact with Chwistek.

World War II

In 1939, at the start of World War II, Galicia was occupied by the Sowjets and became part of the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic (Ukranian SSR>). The medical school of the Jan Kazimierz University became an autonomous institution, and Fleck was made head of its microbiology department. When Germany invaded Galicia in June 1941 (Operation Barbarossa), Fleck lost his position and had to move into the Lwów Ghetto with his wife and his son. At that time, there were roughly 140.000 Jews in Lvov, representing 30% of the population. By autumn 1942, there were only 15.000 Jews left in the ghetto and 12.000 in the labour camp. According to Fleck, roughly 70% of the people in the ghetto became infected with typhus (see Fleck's testimony in Kogon, 1946, cited in Schnelle, 1982: 50-51).

Fleck continued his research in the ghetto's hospital, in spite of great difficulty. He developed a new typhus vaccine from the urine of people infected with the disease. (A short article on his work appeared in the Ghetto Lwow Jewish Gazette on 27 May 1942; see Grzybowski & Ciesielska, 2014.) He published his findings after the war (Fleck, 1946c, 1947). When the Germans realised that one of Europe's typhus experts was working in the Lvov ghetto, they asked him whether the typhus vaccine would also work for Germans. Fleck responded that this was doubtful because Germans were a different race and the vaccine was produced for the urine of Jews. (See Fleck's Yad Vashem testimony. The Germans were so brainwashed by Nazi race theory that they believed it.) In December 1942, Fleck and his family were arrested and moved to the pharmaceutical factory “Laokoon” in the suburb Zniesienie. Fleck and a few other doctors were forced to produce the typhus vaccine there.

In February 1943, Fleck, his familty and his collaborators were arrested again and sent to the Auschwitz concentration camp (Oświęcim). After a while, Fleck started working in Block 10 of the concentration camp, where several imprisoned doctors were given the task to diagnose syphilis (using the Wassermann test), typhus (Weil-Felix test) and other diseases (Gruber-Widal test).

In December 1943 or January 1944, Fleck and his family were moved to the Buchenwald concentration camp, where he was forced to contribute to the development of a typhus vaccine from the lungs of infected mice or rabbits, according to a method developed by the Frenchman Paul Giroud.

In Buchenwald, the German physician and Waffen-SS officer Erwin Ding-Schuler was in charge of developing and testing vaccines for typhus, yellow fever, gas gangrene, smallpox and other diseases. Several doctors, bacteriologists, serologists and chemists had been brought to Buchenwald to develop a typhus vaccine. (The Germans urgently needed a typhus vaccine for their troops on the eastern front line.) Officially, the team produced two types of vaccine: a normal one for the Waffen-SS, and another one that looked a bit cloudy and that could be used for the prisoners. In reality, the team produced a small quantity of first-class typhus vaccine for prisoners that were at risk of getting infected, and a large quantity of a useless (but harmless) vaccine for the Waffen-SS (see Kogon, 1946). They managed to hide this act of sabotage from Erwin Ding-Schuler and the SS in general until the liberation of the camp by the American army in April 1945.

Before Ludwik Fleck's arrival in Block 50 in Buchenwald, the team had been producing what they thought was a mild typhus vaccine. When Fleck noticed that they had confused to Rickettsia bacteria with something else, they asked him not to reveal this to Ding-Schuler. They later managed to produce a proper typhus vaccine after receiving infected mice lungs and intestines from Kraków. This vaccine could only be produced in small quantities. When Ding-Schuler requested large quantities of the typhus vaccine, they produced large quantities of a harmless vaccine. Ding-Schuler did not have any bacteriological knowledge, so he never saw through the trick. Somehow, the SS never noticed that the vaccine was fake (Bayle, 1950: 1178f).
Fleck would later describe the error made by the team as an example of the working of a thought collective (Fleck, 1946f; Schnelle, 1982: 59-63).

Fleck, his wife Ernestina Waldmann and his son Ryszard survived the concentration camps, but his sisters Henryka Fleck-Silber and Antonia Fleck-Kessler and his other relatives did not.

In 1948, Ludwik Fleck travelled to Nuremberg to testify in the IG Farben Trial (part of a series of twelve trials in Nuremberg between 1946 and 1949). His testimony was about the “experiments” to test a typhus vaccine by IG Farben on prisoners in Buchenwald's Block 46 (Schnelle, 1982: 63; Fleck, 2011: 524).

After World War II

In October 1945, Fleck became head of the department for microbiology at the Maria Curie-Skłodowska University in Lublin, which had been founded in October 1944. (In 1946, Lvov became part of the Soviet Union; the Soviets resettled 100,000 to 140,000 Poles from Lvov to the Recovered Territories in the Polish People's Republic.) He obtained his habilitation in 1947. In 1952, he moved to Warsaw where he became head of the Department for Microbiology and Immunology of the state intitute Mother and Child.

One of Fleck's main areas of interest was his discovery about the behaviour of leucocytes during inflammations and other stress situations (pregnancy, serious loss of blood, …). In such situations, leucocytes can clump together; these clumps exhibit a higher mobility and a stronger phagocytic activity. Fleck called this phenomenon leukergy. Based on his findings, he developed the “Fleck test” for the detection of inflammations and infections. He also renewed his collaboration with the mathematician Hugo Steinhaus in order to work on a statistical description of his observations.
Leukergy is not well known in Western medicine. This is probably due to a combination of two factors: Polish medical research was not given great attention in the West, and microbiology after World War II was strongly influenced by the new approaches from molecular biology and biochemistry (Schnelle, 1982: 65).
Note: leukergy is not to be confused with leukocytosis, which refers to a higher leukocyte count in the blood.

Fleck also remained interested in philosophy and psychology and continued publishing articles on philosophy of science. He probably worked on a new book that would constitute the continuation of Entstehung und Entwicklung einer wissenschaftlichen Tatsache. Unfortunately, the manuscript for this new book could not be found after Fleck's death (Schnelle, 1982: 67).

In 1957, Fleck and his wife moved to Israel. The Israeli authorities created a position for him as head of the Department of Experimental Pathology at the Israel Institute for Biological Research, a government defense research institute in Ness-Ziona. Fleck died of heart attack on 5 June 1961.
