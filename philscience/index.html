Why Philosophy of Science

  • Wilkins, John S.: Why do philosophy of science (6 July 2011). Wilkins' reason for “doing” philosophy of science is summed up in the following quote:
    Simply because it is where the epistemic action is: science is where we do get knowledge, and I wish to understand how and why, and the limitations.
    (See also the links to related articles at the bottom of the blogpost.)
  • Blackburn, Simon: Why Philosophy of Science, 15-minute excerpt from a longer interview.
  • Alex Rosenberg, author of Philosophy of Science: A Contemporary Introduction, gave a similar reason to Wilkins':
    Science is the most reliable route to knowledge of the nature of reality. But in our culture and in others, this claim is persistently challenged by those who embrace one or another religious or other nonscientific source that claims to provide real understanding. Evaluating these competing claims is crucial to individual well being—whose guidance should each of us accept? And it’s essential to the guidance of social policy—the design and improvement of institutions that make human life possible and rewarding. The philosophy of science addresses these questions about science’s claim to objectivity and its implicit denial that there are alternative routes to knowledge.
  • Do we need philosophy of science?, discussion on Quora (started in 2011).
  • Vallicella, Bill: Why Do We Need Philosophy?, Maverick Philosopher (blog), 02.05.2013.
    This is not really a discussion about philosophy as such, but about philosophical issues that arise from physics, more specifically the question what “time” is.

Introductory Books

There are many introductions to philosophy of science on the market. Some are very short (e.g. the books by Tetens, Okasha and Lecourt) and primarily aimed at the general public, while others are aimed at students (e.g. Chalmers, Wiltsche and Schülein).

  • Tetens, Holm: Wissenschaftstheorie: Eine Einführung. C. H. Beck, 2013. ISBN 978-3-406-65331-5.
    This short and accessible introduction discusses what science is and what we can and should expect from it. Tetens defines science as an ideal that various disciplines fulfil to varying degrees. Tetens divides the ideal of science in five ideals: the ideals of truth, justification, explanation & understanding, intersubjectivity and reflection (p. 17). The humanities can also fulfil these ideals (chapter 13). The final chapters discuss the perceived superiority of science, the limits to our knowledge about the future, and scientism.
  • Okasha, Samir: Philosophy of Science: A Very Short Introduction. Oxford University Press, 2002. ISBN 978-0-19-280283-5.
    This is another short introduction that is written in a very informal and accessible style. It can be read together with Tetens' book without too much overlap. It also represents a very different concept of science: everybody knows that subjects such as physics, chemistry, and biology constitute science, while subjects such as art, music, and theology do not (p. 1). In spite of this this casual dismissal, the book is highly recommended for readers who want a first introduction to philosophy of science. The book ends with suggestions for further reading for each chapter.
  • Lecourt, Dominique: La philosophie des sciences. Que sais-je? 3624. 5e édition. Paris: Presses Universitaires de France, 2013. (128 p.) ISBN 978-2-13-058053-9.
    This short book takes a more historical approach, although it jumps back a few times in history, for example, it introduces David Hume when discussing the problem of induction in chapter 9. The overview starts in the 19th century, with figures such as William Whewell and Auguste Comte. One of the benefits of this book is that it discusses thinkers that are often ignored in other introductions, for example, Ernst Mach, Gaston Bachelard, Georges Canguilhem and Jean Piaget. (The English and German books in this list generally ignore French philosophy, except for references to Auguste Comte and Pierre Duhem.) Readers of Tetens' or Okasha's introduction can still learn a lot from this book.
  • Eberhard, Kurt: Einführung in die Erkenntnis- und Wissenschaftstheorie. Urban-Taschenbücher 386. Stuttgart: Kohlhammer, 1987.
  • Chalmers, Alan F.: What Is This Thing Called Science? Fourth edition. Indianapolis/Cambridge: Hackett Publishing, 2013 (282 p.) ISBN 978-1-62466-038-2. McGrawHill, 2013 (316 p.) ISBN 978 03 35 26 27 86.
    This is a thorough introduction that covers neopositivism, Karl Raimund Popper, Thomas Kuhn, Imre Lakatos, Paul Feyerabend, Bayesian philosophy of science (which many other introductions don't cover), and realism versus anti-realism. Like most other introductions, it does not consider the humanities as sciences.
    The German translation of this book, Wege der Wissenschaft, is based on the third edition from 1999, not on the fourth and last edition. One of the benefits of this translation is the addition of a German bibliography at the end of the book. This bibliography contains over 300 titles, organised in categories such as reference works, compendiums, general introductions, philosophy of science (and epistemology) in specific schools of philosophy, and philosophy of science for specific disciplines (psychology, economics, law, history, literature, etc.) and bibliographical works.
  • Wiltsche, Harald A.: Einführung in die Wissenschaftstheorie. UTB 3936. Vandenhoeck & Ruprecht, 2013. ISBN 978 38 25 23 93 67.
  • Lewens, Tim: The Meaning of Science: A Pelican Introduction. Pelican, 2015. ISBN 978-0-141-97742-3.
    The first chapters in this book discusses philosophy of science mainly through the theories of Karl R. Popper and Thomas Kuhn; later chapters discuss topics such as the limits of science, the relationship between science and society, and realism versus anti-realism.

Other introductions:

  • Rosenberg, Alex: Philosophy of Science: A Contemporary Introduction. Third edition. Routledge, 2012. (308 p.) ISBN 9780415891776.
    This book is organised by topic (e.g. why do laws explain?; the structure of scientific theories; induction and probability) and may be rather challenging for readers with no background in philosophy.
  • Godfrey-Smith, Peter: Theory and Reality: An Introduction to the Philosophy of Science. University of Chicago Press, 2003. (288 p.) ISBN 9780226300634.
  • Moulines, C. Ulises: La philosophie des sciences: l’invention d’une discipline (fin XIXe-début XXIe siècle). Paris: Éditions Rue d’Ulm/Presses de l’École normale supérieure, 2006. ISBN 2-7288-0357-9.
  • Barker, Gillian; Kitcher, Philip: Philosophy of Science: A New Introduction. Oxford University Press, 2013. (192 pages) ISBN 9780195366198.
  • DeWitt, Richard: Worldviews: An Introduction to the History and Philosophy of Science. Second edition. Wiley-Blackwell, 2010. (392 pages) ISBN 978-1-4051-9563-8.
    According to Joel Ballavian, who teaches or taught the course Philosophy of Science (PHIL3550) at Western Michigan University, this is a very accessible book. The third edition is scheduled for publication in January 2018. Joel Ballavian also recommends the book Inference to the Best Explanation by Peter Lipton.

Introductions Aimed at Specific Audiences

Some introductions to the methods and philosophy of science are aimed at students of specific disciplines, so they are listed separately from the more general introductions (listed above). Some of the books listed here do not have philosophy of science as their main focus.

  • van Schravendijk, Chris: Wetenschappelijk denken. Een inleiding voor de medische en biomedische wetenschappen. Tweede, herziene druk. Leuven: Acco, 2012. (247 p.) ISBN 978-90-334-8933-4.
    This book is not so much an introduction to philosophy of science as an introduction to scientific thinking and practice. The first part explains the development of the scientific worldview, then discusses scientific thinking as a method, as a social phenomenon, and how scientific thinking works in practice. The chapter on scientific thinking as a method discusses Popper; the chapter on science as a social phenomenon discusses Kuhn (and also mentions Ludwik Fleck). The is no discussion of philosophy of science since Kuhn (i.e. nothing since the 1960s). The final chapter is about getting a PhD. Most chapters contain some content that is specific to medicine and biomedicine, but most content has a more general relevance. Some knowledge of medical or biomedical terminology is helpful. Because of its attention to medicine and biomedicine, the book can serve as a complement to other books on philosophy of science, which typically focus on physics.

  • Braunstein, Jean-François, dir.: Textes Clés de l’histoire des sciences. Méthodes, styles et controverses. Textes clés. Vrin, 2008. (384 p.) ISBN 978-2-7116-1933-7.
    This anthology contains texts by G. Bachelard, G. Canguilhem, A. Comte, A. Crombie, L. Daston, P. Duhem, L. Fleck, M. Foucault, A. Koyré, T. Kuhn, P. Laffitte, R.K. Merton and P. Tannery.
  • Gadenne, Volker; Visintin, Aldo, Hrsg.: Wissenschaftsphilosophie. Alber-Texte Philosophie. Freiburg; München: Karl Alber, 1999. (192 p.) ISBN 978-3-495-48005-2.
    A rather short anthology that starts with Francis Bacon's Novum Organum. The authors provide an introduction to each of the texts.
  • Achinstein, Peter, ed.: Science Rules: A Historical Introduction to Scientific Methods. Baltimore & London: Johns Hopkins University Press, 2004. (440 p.) ISBN 978 08 01 87 94 49.
    This book contains selected texts on the following topics: Descartes' rationalism, Newton's inductivism, hypothetico-deductivism (Mill, Whewell, Popper), realism versus anti-realism (Duhem, van Fraassen, Perrin, Salmon) and universal rules of method (Galilei, Feyerabend, Kuhn). The book also contains several essays by Achinstein about most of these topics. The examples of scientific theories in the book all come from physics.

Criticism of Science and Scientism

  • Scientism in Wikipedia. This article discusses various definitions of scientism.
  • Scientism Workshop: a workshop about the concept of scientism, organised by Massimo Pigliucci and Maarten Boudry in May 2014. The presentations can be downloaded from the website.
    The organisers of the workshop pointed out that, There is no agreed upon definition of the term, but as a first approximation, it describes an exaggerated deference towards science, or unwarranted belief in the superiority and universal applicability of scientific methods.
  • Burnett, Thomas: What is Scientism?, BioLogos (blog), 11.06.2012.
    This article provides a short history of scientism.
  • Vallicella, Bill: What is Scientism?, Maverick Philosopher (blog), 24.06.2012.
    This articles gives Vallicella's definition of scientism and comments on an article about scientism by Thomas Burnett. His central argument is the following: If scientism is defined as the thesis that the only genuine knowledge is scientific knowledge (“strong scientism”), and if scientism is a philosophical thesis instead of a scientific thesis, then we cannot know whether scientism is true. In other words, strong scientism does not meet its own criteria for genuine knowledge. In addition, if strong scientism were true, moral judgments such as “torturing children for fun is morally wrong” would not be true.
  • Paul Feyerabend - How to Defend Society Against Science is part of a course on Social and Political Theory by Nicole Pepperell (RMIT University).
  • Rupert Sheldrake: The Science Delusion (and on the RevolutionLoveEvolve channel), a talk given at TEDxWhitechapel in January 2013. This talk is based on ideas expressed in Sheldrake's book The Science Delusion and caused controversy and the video was taken down from the TED website. See Rupert Sheldrake's website and this interview with Alex Tsakiris on Skeptiko 02.04.2013 (also copied here).
    See also Open for discussion: Graham Hancock and Rupert Sheldrake from TEDxWhitechapel (14 March 2013), Graham Hancock and Rupert Sheldrake, a fresh take (18 March 2013) and The debate about Rupert Sheldrake’s talk (19 March 2013) on the TED blog. Other Links

  • Philosophy of Science is the official journal of the Philosophy of Science Association. Non-subscribers can buy single issues as e-book.
    (The journal is published by The University of Chicago Press.)
  • The philosophy of science by the Understanding Science project, is a short article that explains what kinds of questions philosophy of science is concerned with, and lists some major ideas and figures.
  • The category of articles related to philosophy of science in the Internet Encyclopedia of Philosophy. Authors who contribute articles to this encyclopedia must have a PhD in philosophy. The encyclopedia has no general article on philosophy of science.
  • The biochemist Laurence T. Moran has written many blog posts related to philosophy of science. The blog posts cover topics such as methodological naturalism, the demarcation between science and pseudoscience, evolution versus creationism and “intelligent design”, scientism, the possibility of scientific research on the supernatural, and critical thinking.
  • The Prometheus Journal, an undergraduate philosophy journal published by students at Johns Hopkins University, has also published a number of articles on philosophy of science.
  • Berezow, Alex B.; Hartsfield, Tom: What Separates Science from Non-Science?, Real Clear Science, 30.05.2012.
    This is a follow-up to a blogpost in which physicsit Tom Hartsfield asks whether NSF Should Stop Funding Social 'Science' because research should be repeatable, exact and quantitative. Berezow and Hartsfield identify five characteristics of scientifically rigorous studies:
    1. clearly defined terminology
    2. quantifiability
    3. highly controlled conditions
    4. reproducibility
    5. predictability and testability
    Note that the article merely defines a distinction between scientifically rigorous studies and studies that are not scientifically rigorous, while the article title draws a much more radical conclusion.
  • Stevens, Jacqueline: Political Scientists Are Lousy Forecasters, The New York Times, 23.06.2012.
  • Vallicella, Bill: Is Political Science Science?, Maverick Philosopher (blog), 25.06.2012.
    This articles quotes Berezow and Hartsfield's five criteria for science and points out that certain branches of physics do not satisfy all five criteria. He agrees that political science is not science because it is bad at making forecasts. However, he rejects scientism, i.e. the position that only rigorously scientific knowledge constitutes real knowledge.
  • Wilson, Timothy D.: Stop bullying the 'soft' sciences. The social sciences are just that -- sciences, Los Angeles Times, 12.07.2012.
    Psychology professor Timothy D. Wilson defends the position that psychology is a science and points out a number of psychological research outcomes that were the result of carefully controlled experiments on human behaviour. He admits that the social sciences are not perfect but that many people have benefited from its research results.
  • Berezow, Alex B.: Keep Psychology Out of the Science Club, Real Clear Science, 12.07.2012. (Reprinted from Los Angeles Time, 13.07.2012, where it was published in response to Timothy D. Wilson's article.)
    According to Berezow, psychology is not science because it does not meet the five criteria laid out above. For example, happiness research is not scientific because happiness is not clearly defined and cannot be quantified. In addition, psychology does not make reliable predictions about human behaviour. Even though some psychological research is scientifically more rigorous, the field as a whole cannot count as science without redefining the concept of science.
  • Berezow, Alex B.: Why Psychology and Statistics Are Not Science, Real Clear Science, 02.11.2015.
    Berezow says that psychology is important but that one can learn just as much about human behavior by reading Shakespeare, studying religious texts, or contemplating art (which seems to imply that controlled experiments add no value to psychological research). He considers psychology as part of the liberal arts and points out that the truth claims made by the liberal arts are not as strong as those made by physics, chemistry, and biology. The use of mathematics does not turn academic disciplines such as pscyhology and statistics into sciences.
  • Hartsfield, Tom: Statistics Shows Psychology Is Not Science, Real Clear Science, 03.11.2015.
    This is yet another article that explains why psychology does not meet the criteria for science that Berezow and Hartsfield had defined in May 2012. This time, the occasion is a magazine article about the behavioural sciences scandal (October 2015) and a Nature article about the outcomes of a reproducibility test in psychology (April 2015).
  • Thurs, Daniel P.: The Scientific Method is a Myth, The Crux (blog), 28.10.2015. (Excerpt from Newton’s Apple and Other Myths about Science, edited by Ronald L. Numbers & Kostas Kampourakis, Harvard University Press, 2015. ISBN 9780674967984.)
    The author points out that there is no general scientific method that applies across sciences. He borrows Raymond Williams' concept of “keywords”: words or phrases that are used so often that everyone seems to know what they mean. Although most people would be at pains to define the “scientific method”, the flexibility of the term could be a force of cohesion and a tool for inspiring action among groups. The rest of the article gives a brief history of the term “scientific method”
