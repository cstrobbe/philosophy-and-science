In 2005 John P. A. Ioannidis published an article entitled “Why Most Published Research Findings Are False” in the journal PLOS Medicine (2.8 (August 2005): e124). The article led to debate in mass media, on blogs by scientist and in journals, and to follow-up articles and lectures by Ioannidis, for example:

  • John P. A. Ioannidis: An Epidemic of False Claims, Scientific American, 17.05.2011.
  • Why Most Published Research Findings Are False (June 2011) is a video by an anonymous person about Ioannidis' article.
  • Simon Harper: Why Most Published Research Findings are False – Or Are They? (blog), 08.11.2011.
  • John Ioannidis: Why Science Is Not Necessarily Self-Correcting, Perspectives on Psychological Science, 7.6 (November 2012): 645-654.
  • John Ioannidis: The Reliability of Biomedical Evidence and How to Improve It is a 90-minute lecture that Ioannidis gave at Stanford University's Center for Law and the Biosciences on 2 April 2013.
  • Trouble at the Lab, The Economist, 19.10.2013.
  • John Horgan: A Dig Through Old Files Reminds Me Why I’m So Critical of Science (blog), 02.11.2013.
  • Steven Novella: Is Science Broken?, Neurologica Blog, 05.11.2013. (Contains links to other discussions about Ioannidis' article.)
  • Stephen Hsu: Is science self-correcting? (blog), 10 December 2013.
  • John Ioannidis: Reproducible Research: True or False? is an hour-long talk that is part of the series “Talks at Google” (published on YouTube in June 2014).
  • John Ioannidis on Moving Toward Truth in Scientific Research (YouTube, 5'45) on the PLOS Video Channel (January 2015).
  • John Ioannidis: Lessons and Pitfalls from Medical Research, a 30-minute lecture for the Berkeley Initiative for Transparency in the Social Sciences (published 9 January 2015).
  • John P. A. Ioannidis: Reproducible Research: True or False? is an hour-long talk for the Bay Area Skeptics (published in August 2015).
