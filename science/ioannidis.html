In 2005 John P. A. Ioannidis published an article entitled “Why Most Published Research Findings Are False” in the journal PLOS Medicine (2.8 (August 2005): e124). The article led to debate in mass media, on blogs by scientists and in journals, and to follow-up articles and lectures by Ioannidis, for example:

  • Djulbegovic, Benjamin; Hozo, Iztok: When Should Potentially False Research Findings Be Considered Acceptable?, PLoS Medicine, 27.02.2007.
  • Moonesinghe, Ramel; Khoury, Muin J.; Janssens, A. Cecile J. W.: Most Published Research Findings Are False—But a Little Replication Goes a Long Way, PLoS Medicine, 4.2 (February 2007): e28.
  • Hunter, John: How to Deal with False Research Findings, Curious Cat - Science and Engineering Blog, 1 March 2007.
  • Freedman, David H.: Lies, Damned Lies, and Medical Science, The Atlantic, November 2010.
  • Ioannidis, John P. A. : An Epidemic of False Claims, Scientific American, 17.05.2011.
  • Why Most Published Research Findings Are False (June 2011) is a video by an anonymous person about Ioannidis' article.
  • Harper, Simon: Why Most Published Research Findings are False – Or Are They? (blog), 08.11.2011.
  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: The Reliability of Biomedical Evidence and How to Improve It is a 90-minute lecture that Ioannidis gave at Stanford University's Center for Law and the Biosciences on 2 April 2013.
  • Trouble at the Lab, The Economist, 19.10.2013.
  • Horgan, John: A Dig Through Old Files Reminds Me Why I’m So Critical of Science (blog), 02.11.2013.
  • Novella, Steven: Is Science Broken?, Neurologica Blog, 05.11.2013. (Contains links to other discussions about Ioannidis' article.)
  • Konforti, Boyana: In defense of science, Cell.com blog, 18 November 2015.
    This article responds to articles in The Economist and LA Times and cites research at Bayer and Amgen that reported on non-reproducible findings. The author states that these findings cannot be extrapolated to science in general and points out that there are different factors that can contribute to the irreproducibility of research results, such as problems in experimental design, lack of proper controls, lack of technical expertise, flawed statistical analyses, and scientific misconduct.
  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: How to Make More Published Research True, PLOS Medicine 11.10 (October 2014): e1001747. (DOI: 10.1371/journal.pmed.1001747)
    One of the comments on the article points out that Ioannidis' findings are not surprising because of the pressure on researchers: when impact factors became the criterion for “quality assessment”, the entire career of a research became dependent on journal publications, and both the number of publications and the number of journals increased rapidly.
  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: Reproducible Research: True or False? is an hour-long talk that is part of the series “Talks at Google” (published on YouTube in June 2014).
  • Bastian, Hilda: A Stronger Post-Publication Culture Is Needed for Better Science, editorial, PLoS Medicine, December 2014.
  • John Ioannidis on Moving Toward Truth in Scientific Research (YouTube, 5'45) on the PLOS Video Channel (January 2015).
  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: Lessons and Pitfalls from Medical Research, a 30-minute lecture for the Berkeley Initiative for Transparency in the Social Sciences (published 9 January 2015).
  • Belluz, Julia: John Ioannidis has dedicated his life to quantifying how science is broken, Vox, 16 February 2015.
    This is an interview with Ioannidis about what has changed in science in the ten years that passed since the publication of the paper “Why Most Published Research Findings are False”, and what still needs to change (e.g. claims about the significance of findings, how and when papers are reviewed, the incenctive and reward system, the need for research on research, and the importance of research methods in the education of scientists). All in all, Ioannidis is optimistic about progress of science.
  • Boulesteix, Anne-Laure: Ten Simple Rules for Reducing Overoptimistic Reporting in Methodological Computational Research, Editorial, PLoS Computational Biology, 11.4 (April 2015): e1004191.
  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: Reproducible Research: True or False?: an hour-long talk for the Bay Area Skeptics (published in August 2015).

Is Science Self-Correcting?

  • Ioannidis, John P. A.: Why Science Is Not Necessarily Self-Correcting, Perspectives on Psychological Science, 7.6 (November 2012): 645-654. (You can also preview the first few pages of the article on ResearchGate.)
  • Stroebe, Wolfgang; Postmes, Tom; Spears, Russell: Scientific Misconduct and the Myth of Self-Correction in Science, Perspectives on Psychological Science, 7.6 (November 2012): 670-688 . (Also available on Sage Journals.)
  • Estes, Sarah: The Myth of Self-Correcting Science, The Atlantic, 20 December 2012.
  • Stephen Hsu: Is science self-correcting? (blog), 10 December 2013.

Other Articles

Some of the articles below only refer to Ioannidis.

  • Ioannidis, John; Doucouliagos, Chris: What's to Know About the Credibility of Empirical Economics? (PDF), Journal of Economic Surveys (2013) Vol. 00, No. 00, pp. 1–8.
    This article is about the empirical evidence on the lack of a robust reproducibility culture in economics and business research, the prevalence of potential publication and other selective reporting biases and related issues in economics research.
  • Frederik, Jesse: Waarom 90 procent van het economisch onderzoek de prullenbak in kan, De correspondent, 8 September 2015. (“Why 90% of all economic research findings can be chucked out”)
