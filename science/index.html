This page collects mostly non-academic resources about science.

YouTube Channels and Videos

  • Colin Frayn: Understanding Science: a series over 40 videos (each between 10 and 40 minutes long) about various sciences and how science works.
  • Sense About Science: the YouTube channel of the British charity Sense about Science.
  • Think Like a Scientist is a playlist of videos by the Open University.
  • Nature Video: the YouTube channel of the British scientific journal Nature.
  • Scientific American: the YouTube channel of the American science magazine Scientific American.
  • Spektrum Verlag: the YouTube channel of Spektrum der Wissenschaft, the German edition of Scientific American.
  • New Scientist: the YouTube channel of the British science magazine New Scientist.
  • MinutePhysics: a YouTube channel with short videos about physics and other sciences. It even has an astrononmically correct version of “Twinkle twinkle”.
  • het denkgelag is the channel of a Belgian non-profit organization that brings together interesting speakers to stimulate public discussion on science and reason and to foster critical thinking. The organisation's website is available at www.denkgelag.be.
  • Richard Feynman: Cargo Cult Science. The physicist Richard Feynman (1918-1988) invented the term “cargo cult science” for his 1974 inaugural lecture at the California Institute of Technology. The text of the lecture was later included in his book Surely You're Joking, Mr. Feynman! (1985), but it is also available on Caltech's website (and elsewhere).
    Wikipedia has an article about Millikan's oil drop experiment, which Feynman mentions in his lecture. However, nobody has been able to identify the research on rats that Feynman attributes to a certain Mr. Young.

For a funny take on science journalism or science itself, see Last Week Tonight with John Oliver: Scientific Studies (HBO) (20 minutes, YouTube).

About Science in General

  • William M.K. Trochim: Research Methods Knowledge Base: a comprehensive web-based textbook that addresses all of the topics in a typical introductory undergraduate or graduate course in social research methods. A printed version is also available.
  • AAAS: Science for All Americans. This book about science literarcy is one of the outcomes from the AAAS's Project 2061. According to the book description, is is primarely meant for educators, parents, school administrators, and policymakers. Oxford University Press published a printed version in 1991.
  • The World Science Festival is a yearly event in New York City produced by the Science Festival Foundation (a non-profit organisation). World Science U is a related website that provides education on science. The Festival also has a YouTube channel.
  • Sciences Essonne is a network of universities, research centres, associations, companies etc. in the Essonne department in the north of France, that aims to bring science to the general public.
  • Angier, Natalie: The Canon: The Beautiful Basics of Science. Faber & Faber, 2008. (ISBN 9780571239726; 304 pages). For a list of scientists interviewed by Angier, see the Wikipedia article about the book.
  • Goldacre, Ben: Bad Science. Fourth Estate, 2008. This book criticises media reporting on health and science issues. See also Goldacre's TED talk Battling Bad Science (TED talk, 2011).
  • Shermer, Michael: Science Friction: Where the Known Meets the Unknown. Times Books, 2004. (See also the Wikipedia article about Science Friction.)
  • Isaac Physics: Mastering Physics by Solving Problems: from School to University!

Registering All Medical Trials

The British physician Ben Goldacre founded the AllTrials campaign to require open science practices in clinical trials. See www.alltrials.net. The AllTrials website explains why this initiative matters. The main reason is that trials with negative results are twice as likely to remain unreported as those with positive results so doctors and other people who make decisions about medicines don’t have full information about the benefits and risks of treatments we use every day. For this reason, each clinical trial should be registered before it starts. One of the benefits is that it should be easier to find out whether research results are being withheld. Anyone can sign the petition to make a registry of all trials a reality.

Science Denial and Anti-Science

The most well-known examples of science denial in the Western world are creationism and climate change denial, which are particularly strong in the USA.

  • Mooney, Chris C.: The Republican War on Science. Basic Books, 2005. See also the book website, Wikipedia article, lecture recorded by UCTV (24 April 2006, 29 minutes), and the 2014 Hans Jenny Memorial Lecture The Science of Why We Don't Believe in Science (2 April 2014).
  • Mashey, John: The machinery of climate anti-science, lecture that the University of Victoria's Pacific Institute for Climate Solutions on 7 April 2011 (85 minutes, uploaded on 28 August 2012).
  • Krauss, Lawrence: American Perspectives: Scientific Literacy & Public Policy: recording of a lecture at the American Enterprise Institute in February 2012 (84 minutes, uploaded on 26 March 2017).
  • Berezow, Alex B.: Science left behind: Feel-good fallacies and the rise of the anti-scientific left, recording of a lecture at the American Enterprise Institute on 10 October 2012. (See also Science left behind: Feel-good fallacies and the rise of anti-science on YouTube.)
  • Secular Talk: School Apologizes For Teaching Science In Science Class (YouTube, 29 September 2015, 4 minutes). This video is based on the article Florida school apologizes for warning students that evolution deniers were trying to ‘mislead’ them by David Ferguson (Raw Story, 28 September 2015). A science teacher at Heritage Middle School in Daytona, Florida, had give a lesson or assignment titled “Not Just a Theory” in which he/she pointed out that people who deny evolution be saying it is “just a theory” don't use the word “theory” in its scientific sense and are probably trying to mislead other people. After parents complained about this, the school issued an apology.
  • The Young Turks: Kids Will Now Take "Anti-Science" Classes (YouTube, 27 May 2017, 7 minutes). The video discusses a law in Florida.
