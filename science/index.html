Science is neither a philosophy nor a belief system. It is a combination of mental operations that has become increasingly the habit of educated peoples, a culture of illuminations hit upon by a fortunate turn of history that yielded the most effective way of learning about the real world ever conceived. E. O. Wilson: Consilience: The Unity of Knowledge (1988).

This page collects mostly non-academic resources about science.

Registering All Medical Trials

The British physician Ben Goldacre founded the AllTrials campaign to require open science practices in clinical trials. See www.alltrials.net. The AllTrials website explains why this initiative matters. The main reason is that trials with negative results are twice as likely to remain unreported as those with positive results so doctors and other people who make decisions about medicines don’t have full information about the benefits and risks of treatments we use every day. For this reason, each clinical trial should be registered before it starts. One of the benefits is that it should be easier to find out whether research results are being withheld. Anyone can sign the petition to make a registry of all trials a reality.

  • How to learn Quantum Mechanics on your own (a self-study guide) (Looking Glass Universe on YouTube, 10 minutes, 08.08.2019). This is a video in which Mithuna Yoganathan, a PhD student studying quantum computing, proposes a curriculum that allows you to learn quantum mechanics without too many mathematical prerequisites. One book that she would recommend to everyone, from complete beginners to physics pros is volume three of The Feynman Lectures on Physics: instead of starting with the required mathematics, Richard Feynman starts with the intuition, which makes it easier to learn the mathematics. The relevant mathematics relies a lot on calculus. Mithuna suggests a way that uses linear algebra instead, which is much easier. The relevant algebra can be learnt from a few selected chapters from Gilbert Strang's book Introduction to Linear Algebra. After this, you should be ready for the two quantum mechanics books she recommends: Quantum Mechanics: The Theoretical Minimum by Leonard Susskind and Art Friedman, and A Modern Approach to Quantum Mechanics by John S. Townsend. These books complement each other. For more details and links, see the blog post How to learn quantum mechanics on your own (08.06.2019).
