  • Raval, Siraj: How to Learn Mathematics Fast (Siraj Raval on YouTube, 11 minutes, 19.01.2018).
  • Learn Mathematics from START to FINISH (The Math Sorcerer on YouTube, 18 minutes, 09.12.2020).
    As a starting point, this video recommends the following books: Discrete Mathematics with Applications by Susanna Epp (which requires no previous knowledge of algebra), Discrete Mathematical Structures by Kolman, Busby and Ross, Mathematical Proofs: A Transition to Advanced Mathematics by Chartrand, Polimeni and Zhang (which is much more advanced than the previous books, but you don't need to understand everything), and An Introduction to Abstract Mathematics by Bond and Keane (which is also about proof writing).
    If you need to prepare for learning algebra, the video creator recommends Pre-Algebra from AGS (which contains solutions) and Fearon's Pre-Algebra (which does not contain solutions but which is otherwise very good).
    Next, you can pick College Algebra by Jerome E. Kaufmann or College Algebra Essentials by Blitzer (which is somewhat less beginner friendly than Kaufmann's). This could be followed by A Graphical Approach to Algebra & Trigonometry by Hornsby, Lial and Rockswold (which is often used to teach pre-calculus and trigonometry in the USA; the instructor's edition also contains answers).
    For calculus, there is Calculus by James Stewart (which is popular in the USA and Canada and which contains many problems) and Calculus by Michael Spivak (which contains less material than Stewart's book and is more advanced because it assumes more knowledge of set theory and proof writing).
    After these books, you can move on to A First Course in Differential Equations (with Modeling Applications) by Zill or Ordinary Differential Equations (With Applications) by Larry C. Andrews (which is less popular but good for beginners). Then you can progress to Elementary Linear Algebra Howard Anton (which is good for beginners), Linear Algebra by Friedberg, Insel and Spence (which is more rigorous and harder and proof based) and Linear Algebra by Serge Lang.
    After these books the world is open since you can now learn almost any mathematics that is taught at an undergraduate level. Mathematical Statistics with Applications by Wackerly, Mendenhall and Scheaffer (which is very solid and requires calculus), and A First Course in Probability by Sheldon Ross (which requires a bit of calculus). You can also learn from Fundamentals of Complex Analysis with Applications to Engineering and Science Saff and Snider, and Complex Variables and Applications by Brown and Churchill.
    Real analysis is often considered the bane of math majors. For self study you can use (after learning linear algebra) Analysis I and II by Terence Tao (which are very good for reading and contain good examples), Advanced Calculus: A Course in Mathematical Analysis by Patrick M. Fitzpatrick (which is good for beginners in analysis) and Principles of Mathematical Analysis by Walter Rudin. Also good: Elementary Analysis: The Theory of Calculus by Ross (which spends a lot of time on proofs).
    Abstract Algebra: A First Course by Dan Saracino and Contemporary Abstract Algebra by Joseph Gallian, which are both proof based.
    Other topics you can take up: Introduction to Topology by Gamelin and Greene (which contains solutions, published by Dover Books and therefore cheap), Applied Combinatorics by Alan Tucker (some of which was already covered by the books on discrete mathematics), Naive Set Theory by Paul Halmos (the classic book on the subject, which is fairly short and very enjoyable), Introductory Functional Analysis with Applications by Kreyszig (which requires familiarity with proofs) and Graph Theory by Ronald Gould.
    For higher-level mathematics, see, for example, Real Analysis by H. L. Royden and Real and Complex Analysis by Rudin (both of which are famous and difficult).
    Some other books not mentioned before are Linear Algebra by Hoffman and Kunz (a classic book that was used at MIT and which is proof based), Algebra by Michael Artin (which takes a linear-algebra approach to abstract algebra), Calculus Made Easy by Thompson (which is very old and something you can read while lying in bed), Geometry by Jurgensen, Brown and King (which covers basic geometry and can be learnt independently of the other topics in this video), Finite-Dimensional Vector Spaces by Paul Halmos (a linear-algebra book), Linear Algebra by Lipschutz and Lipson, and Linear Algebra and Its Applications by Gilbert Strang.

  • Hoekstra, Rink; Morey, Richard D.; Rouder, Jeffrey N.; Wagenmakers, Eric-Jan: Robust misinterpretation of confidence intervals, Psychonomic Bulletin & Review, October 2014, Volume 21, Issue 5, pp 1157–1164.
    This is a brief report on a study on how researchers and students in the field of psychology interpret six particular statements about confidence intervals (CI). The authors found that researchers hardly outperformed the students, even though the students had not received any education on statistical inference whatsoever. The report's abstract concludes with the following statement:
    Our findings suggest that many researchers do not know the correct interpretation of a CI. The misunderstandings surrounding p-values and CIs are particularly unfortunate because they constitute the main tools by which psychologists draw conclusions from data.
