IBM promoted the collection to researchers as a progressive step toward reducing bias in facial recognition. John Smith, who oversees AI research at IBM, said that the company was committed to “protecting the privacy of individuals” and “will work with anyone who requests a URL to be removed from the dataset.” (In other words, IBM thinks that making their use of the photos an opt-out process instead of an opt-in process is sufficient “ethical”.) The articles goes on to say, Despite IBM’s assurances that Flickr users can opt out of the database, NBC News discovered that it’s almost impossible to get photos removed. IBM requires photographers to email links to photos they want removed, but the company has not publicly shared the list of Flickr users and photos included in the dataset, so there is no easy way of finding out whose photos are included. IBM did not respond to questions about this process. The journalist also talked with Woody Hartzog, a professor of law and computer science at Northeastern University, who said Facial recognition can be incredibly harmful when it’s inaccurate and incredibly oppressive the more accurate it gets.

  • Boran, Marie: ‘Short window’ to stop AI taking control of society, warns ex-Google employee, Irish Times, 05.12.2019.
    Meredith Whittaker, one of the original organisers of Google Walkout for Real Change, gives the following warning: What’s frightening about AI isn’t terminators and super intelligent machines: it’s the way AI works to centralise knowledge and power in the hands of those who already have it and further disempower those who don’t. She also points out that ImageNet, a dataset used for image classification, consists of over 15 million images scraped from Flickr and the web without consent, then labelled by low-paid Amazon Mechanical Turk workers. Another issue is the dominance of three US-based companies in the infrastructure domain: Ask any AI start-up and they will tell you they rent their computational infrastructure from one of the Big Three tech companies – Amazon, Microsoft or Google, in that order. They’re often at a loss for where to get their own data, so from this perspective, we can understand the popular commodification of internet technologies that began in the 1990s as creating the conditions for the AI of today. The concentration of datasets and infrastructure has created a power bias: Only five or so companies in the West have the resources to develop this technology at scale, which means that we cannot talk about AI without confronting issues of power. And frankly, we cannot talk about AI without talking about neoliberal capitalism: these systems are already being quietly integrated throughout our social institutions and we’re seeing the costs.

    In order to really understand machine learning, you need to be confident in linear algebra, calculus, probability and statistics. Linear algebra involves the manipulation of matrices. You need descriptive statistics to better understand the data, normalise the data, etcetera. When you have built a model, you need calculus to reduce the arrow function. Giles presents several resources for learning these skills, for example the book Mathematical Methods for Science Students by G. Stephenson (1961; good for linear algebra and calculus), Think Stats: Exploratory Data Analysis in Python by Allen B. Downey (a very accessible introduction to Python and statistics, freely available online), All of Statistics: A Concise Course in Statistical Inference by Larry Wasserman (2004; a more advanced and more expensive book), Essentials of Metaheuristics by Sean Luke (which covers much of machine learning), the YouTube playlist Essence of linear algebra (14 videos on the channel 3Blue1Brown), the YouTube playlist Essence of calculus (12 videos on the channel 3Blue1Brown), the course Mathematics for Machine Learning by Imperial College London, hosted on Coursera, the course Linear Algebra by professor Gilbert Strang on MIT OpenCourseWare (34 video lectures), the course Prediction: Machine Learning and Statistics by professor Cynthia Rudin on MIT OpenCourseWare (not a video course), the paper A Few Useful Things to Know About Machine Learning by Pedro Domingos, the Machine Learning Crash Course by Google (which focuses on TensorFlow), the book Artificial Intelligence: A Modern Approach by Stuart Russell and Peter Norvig, An Introduction to Statistical Learning with Applications in R by Gareth James, Daniela Witten, Trevor Hastie and Robert Tibshirani (downloadable in PDF format).
  • Alva: Machine Learning Books for Beginners (Alva Liu on YouTube, 7 minutes, 19.04.2018).

Online Courses on Artificial Intelligence

  • Digital Skills: Artificial Intelligence: an online course by Accenture hosted on FutureLearn. The course requires an estimated 2 hours of study per week over the course of three weeks. The first run started on 28 October 2019.
  • MBA with Artificial Intelligence Specialism: an online degree by Coventry University that consists of eleven courses starting in September 2018.
  • Artificial Intelligence: Distinguishing Between Fact and Fiction: an online course by Coventry University hosted on FutureLearn. The course requires an estimated 3 hours of study per week over the course of two weeks. The first run is scheduled to start on 20 January 2020.
  • Big Data: Statistical Inference and Machine Learning: online courses by Queensland University of Technology (QUT) hosted on FutureLearn. It is a three-week course that requires 2 hours of study per week. It requires tools such as R, R Studio, H20 Flow and Weka. The course is part of the Big Data Analytics program.
  • Statistics and R is an online course created by Harvard University that is hosted on edX. It is described as An introduction to basic statistical concepts and R programming skills necessary for analyzing data in the life sciences. It is a four-week course that requires two to four hours of study per week. The course is part of the Data Analysis for Life Sciences series.
  • Advanced Machine Learning: online course by The Open University (UK) and Persontyle that is hosted on FutureLearn. It is a four-week course that requires four hours of study per week. Some previous experience with machine learning and statistical modeling is expected. The course uses R.
  • Python Basics for Data Science is an online course created by IBM that is hosted on edX. It is a one-week course that requires 2 to 5 hours of study.
  • Machine Learning with Python: A Practical Introduction is an online course created by IBM and hosted on edX. It is a two-week course that requires 3 to 6 hours of study per week. The course requires some previous experience with Python and data science, e.g. through the course “Python Basics for Data Science”.
  • Deep Learning Fundamentals with Keras is an online course created by IBM that is hosted on edX. It is a five-week course that requires two to four hours of study per week. Keras is an open-source neural network library written in Python.
  • Artificial Intelligence (AI) is an online course created by Columbia University and is hosted on edX. It is a twelve-week course that requires eight to ten hours of study per week and expects previous experience with Python and an understanding of probability, linear algebra and calculus.
  • Data Science: Machine Learning is an online course created by Harvard University that is hosted on edX. It is a four-week course that requires two to four hours of study per week. It is part of Harvard University's Professional Certificate Program in Data Science and assumes that learners have taken the preceding (seven) courses in the program.
  • Machine Learning Foundations: A Case Study Approach is an online course created by the University of Washington and is hosted on Coursera. Is is the first course in the Machine Learning Specialization and requires five to eight hours of study per week over a period of six weeks.
  • Machine Learning is an online course by Andrew Ng (professor at Stanford University and co-founder of Coursera) that is hosted on Coursera. It consists of eleven sections that take between two and eight hours to complete (depending on the section). The course prerequisites are not described.
  • Neural Networks and Deep Learning is an online course created by deeplearning.ai and hosted on Coursera. It is the first course in a Deep Learning Specialization and requires three to six hours per week over a period of four weeks. The course requires basic Python programming skills, mathematics knowledge (matrix vector operations) and basic machine learning knowledge (how to frame a machine learning problem).
